Harm, Stress & Trauma

How characters take damage, resist it, and what breaks when they've taken too much.

Harm

The Four Levels
  • Level 1 — Lesser. Minor injury, wound, or setback. The character takes −1d on rolls using the relevant attribute or action while the harm persists. Two slots. Examples: bruised ribs, rattled nerves, a cut hand, a mild concussion.
  • Level 2 — Moderate. Serious injury. The character takes −1d on all action rolls while this harm persists. Two slots. Examples: broken arm, deep laceration, partial hearing loss from a blast, badly sprained ankle.
  • Level 3 — Severe. The character is incapacitated in the relevant area — they need desperate effort to push through it. One slot. Examples: broken leg, severe burns, a serious stab wound to the torso.
  • Level 4 — Fatal. The character is dead or dying and will die without immediate intervention. There is no mechanical slot for this — it is a narrative judgment. Reserve it for desperate circumstances where the fiction clearly demands it.
How to Assign Harm

Base harm level on position: Controlled → Level 1, Risky → Level 2, Desperate → Level 3. Adjust based on the fiction — a knife thrust from a skilled opponent at risky position might still be Level 3 if the circumstances clearly demand it. Armor can reduce harm by one level (standard armor) or negate it once (special armor). Name the harm specifically — not just "Level 2" but "a blade across the ribs." The name matters for fiction and for recovery.

When all harm slots at a level are filled, overflow pushes to the next level. If all slots are filled and there's nowhere to go, the character is in serious trouble — this is a good moment for an "out of action" consequence rather than immediate death, giving them a chance to resist or for allies to intervene.

Stress

What Stress Is

Stress is the mental and physical strain of operating at the edge of human capability. Characters have a stress track of 9. Stress is spent to gain advantages — it is not purely a damage track. The question is always whether the advantage is worth the cost.

Ways to Gain Stress
  • Push yourself — Spend 2 stress to gain +1d on a roll, or to accomplish a superhuman feat.
  • Assist — Spend 1 stress to give an ally +1d on their roll.
  • Lead a group action — Take stress for each roll that fails below yours.
  • Resistance roll — Take stress equal to 6 minus your highest die to resist a consequence.
  • Rituals and abilities — Many special abilities have stress costs.
Resistance Rolls — Detail

When a character faces a consequence, the player can roll their relevant attribute to resist it. Roll the attribute's full pool (all three action ratings combined). Take 6 minus the highest die as stress. On a critical (two 6s), take 0 stress. Work out what "resisting" means in the fiction first — resisting harm might mean they dodged or took a glancing blow; resisting a complication might mean they spotted the problem in time. A resistance roll can reduce the severity of harm by one level or negate a complication entirely.

Trauma

Taking Trauma

When a character fills their stress track (reaches 9) and must take more stress, they take a trauma condition instead and clear all stress back to 0. They can continue the scene but take the condition's effects immediately. A character with 4 trauma conditions retires — they leave active play, either dead, broken, or changed beyond recognition.

Trauma Conditions

Each condition represents a psychological break. The player chooses which one fits the character's current circumstances. Trauma conditions are permanent — they do not heal. They shape the character's behavior and create complications in play. Lean into them; they are some of the most interesting character material in the game.

  • Cold — You detach to survive. You have no interest in emotional bonds.
  • Haunted — You are visited by visions of those you've hurt or lost. Ghosts feel closer.
  • Obsessed — You are fixated on something to the exclusion of all else.
  • Paranoid — You cannot trust anyone. You see threats everywhere.
  • Reckless — You have stopped fearing consequences. This reads as bravery from the outside.
  • Skittish — You startle easily. Loud sounds, sudden movements, certain smells — they all take you back.
  • Soft — You have lost your edge. Cruelty is harder. Violence costs more.
  • Unstable — Your emotional state swings without warning.

Recovery

Healing Harm in Downtime

Recovery is a downtime activity. A character who spends a downtime action recovering starts or advances their healing clock — a 4-segment clock for Level 1–2 harm, a 6-segment clock for Level 3 harm. Each downtime recovery action ticks the clock by the result of a Recover roll (Constitution/Prowess as the relevant attribute, or just a fortune roll based on the quality of care). When the clock fills, the harm clears. A skilled physicker helping ticks an extra segment.

Emergency Treatment

During a score, a character can attempt to treat another's harm with a Tinker or Study roll — this doesn't clear harm but can stabilize someone who is dying, reduce a Level 3 to Level 2 for the rest of the scene, or buy time. This is not recovery — it is damage control. Recovery requires downtime.

Trauma never heals. Harm heals with time and care. The difference matters — a character with three trauma conditions has been permanently changed by what they've experienced, and the game should reflect that. Players sometimes try to "fix" trauma through narrative; let them try, but the condition stays on the sheet and in the fiction.

Armor

Using Armor

A character with relevant armor can check a box to reduce harm by one level — Level 3 becomes Level 2, Level 2 becomes Level 1. Standard armor has one box. Heavy armor has two. Special armor (an ability or situational protection) can negate harm entirely for a specific type of threat. Armor is checked when used and resets at the start of the next score's downtime. A character has to actually be wearing the armor for it to apply — worth confirming before the situation arises, not after.
