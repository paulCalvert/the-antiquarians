Running the Game

The action roll, position and effect, consequences, and how to keep the fiction moving.

The Action Roll

When to Call for a Roll

Only call for a roll when the outcome is uncertain AND failure is interesting. If success is guaranteed (not challenging enough) or failure just stops the scene dead (not interesting), don't roll — just narrate the result. The roll is for situations where both success and failure open up different, engaging possibilities. Ask yourself: what happens if they fail? If the answer is boring, don't roll.

Building the Dice Pool

The player rolls a number of d6 equal to their action rating (1–4 dots). If the rating is 0, they roll 2d6 and take the lowest result. Apply bonuses before rolling: push yourself (+1d, costs 2 stress), accept a devil's bargain (+1d, GM names a cost), get assistance (+1d, helper takes 1 stress). Take the single highest die result.

Results
  • 1–3 — Bad outcome. Things go wrong. The character doesn't achieve their goal and faces a consequence.
  • 4–5 — Partial success. They succeed, but with a consequence. Or they succeed at a reduced level of effect. Or they get what they wanted but something else goes wrong.
  • 6 — Full success. They do it. No consequence (though you can still introduce complications from the fiction).
  • Critical (multiple 6s) — Exceptional success. They do it with superior effect. Something extra goes right.

Position & Effect

Set Before the Roll

Always establish position and effect before dice hit the table. This is not negotiable — players need to know what they're risking and what they can achieve. Announce it clearly: "This is risky, standard effect." Players can then decide whether to push for better position/effect or accept the terms.

Position — How Dangerous Is This?
  • Controlled — Low risk. You have the upper hand. Failure means a complication or cost, not disaster. Consequences are mild.
  • Risky — The default. You're in danger. Failure means serious consequences. This is most action rolls.
  • Desperate — You're in serious trouble. Failure means severe consequences. Even partial success may come at a heavy cost. Also marks XP for the attribute used.
Effect — How Much Do They Accomplish?
  • Great — More than expected. Tick two segments on a clock, neutralize a threat fully, accomplish the goal with extras.
  • Standard — Normal accomplishment. One segment, one obstacle cleared, one thing done.
  • Limited — Partial accomplishment. Half a segment, a reduced version of the goal, they get something but not everything.
  • Zero — They can't meaningfully affect this at all with this approach. Tell them before they roll; don't waste their action.

Factors that affect effect: Potency (is their tool/approach well-suited?), Scale (how many are they against?), Quality (their equipment vs. the opposition's). Each factor shifts effect up or down. State your reasoning briefly — players should understand why effect is limited before they commit.

Consequences

Types of Consequences
  • Harm — A character is hurt. Apply harm at a level appropriate to the fiction and position. Controlled = Level 1. Risky = Level 2. Desperate = Level 3.
  • Complication — A new threat emerges, a clock ticks, a faction notices, a resource is depleted.
  • Reduced Effect — They succeed, but accomplish less. Good for 4–5 results where you want success-with-cost rather than a setback.
  • Worse Position — The situation deteriorates. Their next action will be at desperate instead of risky, for example.
  • Lost Opportunity — The window closes. They can't try the same approach again — the situation has changed.
Resistance Rolls

A player can always resist a consequence by rolling their relevant attribute (Insight, Prowess, Resolve) and taking stress equal to 6 minus the highest die. On a critical (two 6s), they take 0 stress. This can reduce or negate the consequence entirely — work out what "resisting" it means in the fiction first. Resistance happens after the outcome is announced but before it takes full effect. Don't forget to offer it — players sometimes don't ask.

The golden rule: Consequences should feel earned, not punitive. A bad roll on a desperate action means something went badly wrong — make it feel that way. A 4–5 on a risky roll means partial success — let them have the success, then add the cost. Don't use consequences to invalidate player choices; use them to complicate them.

Other Roll Types

Fortune Rolls

Used when you need to determine an outcome that isn't a character action: does the fence have what they need? Does the patrol pass by? How well does this NPC perform their job? Build a pool of d6 based on how favorable the circumstances are (1d for poor odds, 3d for good odds) and apply the 1–3/4–5/6 scale. Fortune rolls don't have position or effect — just outcome quality. Use them sparingly to resolve offscreen events, not as a substitute for player agency.

Flashbacks

Any player can call a flashback at any time to establish something their character did before the current moment. Brief, plausible actions cost 0 stress. Reasonable preparations cost 1 stress. Complicated setups or things that stretch plausibility cost 2 stress. If what they describe requires a roll, make the roll in the flashback. Flashbacks are a powerful tool — they let players retroactively be competent. Don't deny them; use stress cost to calibrate ambition.

Gathering Information

When players want to know something, they can roll to gather information with an appropriate action. The quality of the result determines how much they learn — on a 6, they get exactly what they asked for plus something useful they didn't know to ask. On a 4–5, they get partial or less actionable information. On a 1–3, they get wrong or incomplete information and possibly a complication. Gathering information well is a skill; reward players who ask specific, smart questions.

The Engagement Roll

Starting a Score

At the start of every score, after the players describe their plan and approach, roll the engagement roll to determine where the scene opens. Base pool is 1d6. Adjust up for a well-suited plan, good information, appropriate gear, or favorable circumstances. Adjust down for alarm, opposition preparation, bad intel, or an approach that doesn't fit. The result sets the initial position:

  • Critical — Ideal circumstances. Open in a controlled position with an advantage.
  • 6 — Good start. Open in a controlled position.
  • 4–5 — Standard start. Open in a risky position.
  • 1–3 — Bad start. Open in a desperate position, or a complication has already occurred.

The engagement roll is not about whether the plan works — it's about where the plan puts you when the action starts. A great engagement roll on a terrible plan just means the terrible plan is already in motion with no easy exit. Don't protect players from their plans; let the plan reveal its flaws through play.
