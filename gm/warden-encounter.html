Wren

A Spirit Warden operating off-book. She has followed the disturbance trail to the Sunken City. She arrives after Session 7.

Who She Is

Physical Description

Late 30s. Slight build that reads more capable than it appears — she moves like someone who has learned to move carefully in spaces where wrong steps have consequences. Short-cropped dark hair, practical, no vanity. Eyes that catch light slightly wrong in low conditions — an occupational side effect of years working in ghost-contaminated environments, not supernatural, just accumulated exposure. She cannot always tell what she is seeing and what she is remembering. A faint chemical smell she cannot fully scrub away: electroplasm residue, mineral and faintly sweet.

In plain clothing she is deliberately unplaceable — not remarkable enough to be remembered. When she arrives at the Sunken City she carries a small worn leather investigation case. Her mask is in a bag at her side, not worn. Setting it aside was a deliberate choice. She is saying: I am not here officially.

The Alias — "Wren"

Spirit Warden designations are institutional — a category and a number. She does not give the crew her designation. She gives them a name she chose herself: Wren. Unofficial. Not filed anywhere. The choice of a real word rather than a code signals something about how she is approaching this meeting. She wants to be remembered as a person.

She has been the Spirit Wardens' de facto investigator for anomalous and non-standard disturbances for six years — the cases nobody else wanted, the things that didn't fit the training. She is very good at her job. She has seen enough strange things that the ghost field no longer surprises her. The harbor does.

What She Knows
  • The ghost train activation — she investigated the aftermath on the 5th Line
  • The sunken temple breach — disturbance readings in the dock district spiked after Session 7
  • The crew's general profile — enough to find them, not enough to name everyone
  • Something old is activating in the harbor at a scale she has not seen before
  • Setarra's name has surfaced twice in adjacent investigation files — she doesn't know the crew's connection
  • The outfit theft — yes. She is holding this deliberately.
What She Does Not Know
  • The harbor compact — she has no framework for what she is detecting
  • The Watcher — she knows something vast is present, not what it is
  • Scurlock's role — his name is not yet connected to anything in her files
  • The three ritual items
  • That the Conductor has been engineering her investigation toward this meeting

How she found the Sunken City: She'd describe it as good detective work — a misfiled document, an informant who suddenly remembered something, a lead that came together faster than it should have. If anyone examines how she got here, the trail is subtly wrong. Too clean. Too convenient. The Conductor has been showing her the next door to open for months. She doesn't know this.

The Arrival

How She Comes In

She doesn't knock with authority. She comes in like a regular customer — examines something on the shelf for a moment, waits for the shop to be empty, and then says to Marge quietly: "I need five minutes with whoever runs this place."

When whoever comes down, her first words before they can speak: "I'm not here about the uniform."

Let that land before she continues. She knows the outfit is here. She's known for a while. She has chosen, deliberately, not to report it. She is announcing that choice as an opening offer — not a threat, not a favor. A signal that she operates outside normal procedure when the situation requires it. Which it does.

What She Asks For

She describes what she has been tracking: disturbances on the 5th Line, the dock district anomalies, the specific spike after the temple breach. She doesn't tell them what she thinks it means — she asks what they know. She wants to understand what the crew has actually been inside.

She is not interrogating. She asks questions the way someone does who has learned to wait for the answer they actually need rather than the one being offered. She watches faces more than she listens to words.

What she wants: a working relationship with people who have been inside whatever is happening. In exchange: she manages the Spirit Warden investigation trail away from them, shares what her organization's sensors are detecting, and holds the outfit case in indefinite suspension.

Branch 1 — Tight-Lipped

The crew listens, gives nothing. They acknowledge being in the areas she describes — hired work, they say, nothing more. They don't name Scurlock, the compact, Setarra. They give her the shape of a crew that knows it's being looked at and is waiting to see what she does.

How She Responds

She reads the room. She's good at reading rooms. She doesn't push — that is not her approach. Pressure doesn't work on people who have chosen silence; it just confirms that silence was right.

She takes a small folded card from her case and sets it on the counter. A location, a code phrase, a message drop. "If you find something you can't handle alone, that's how you reach me. I won't report what I know. I'm not asking for anything right now."

She takes her bag. She leaves.

Long-Term Consequences

She keeps investigating independently. The Conductor keeps nudging her. Her trail and the crew's trail continue to converge — she will have more information each time they cross paths, and each crossing means she's figured out more on her own. The relationship is latent but the clock is ticking.

The risk: if Setarra's network figures out she's investigating — which is a real clock — she becomes a liability before she becomes an asset. She's operating alone. If something happens to her, nobody is looking for her.

The opportunity: the crew holds the card. They can reach her when they choose. On their terms, at a time of their choosing, with whatever they've decided to reveal. That's a meaningful card to hold.

Branch 2 — Alaric Blurts

The conversation has been controlled — until it isn't. She says something specific that lands too close. Maybe she mentions that the disturbance pattern looks like something old activating under obligation, and Alaric, who has been managing the Dalmorrian revelation since the temple, over-corrects. Maybe she mentions that Setarra's name has surfaced in adjacent cases and his face changes half a second before the rest of the crew can cover him. Either way: he gives her something before the crew shuts it down.

The Tell

It doesn't have to be much. A name — Setarra, or Scurlock — spoken or confirmed. A reaction that tells her someone in this room knows exactly what she's been circling. The tell is enough. She's been doing this for six years.

Her reaction: stillness. Not excitement, not alarm. The very specific focused stillness of someone who just received confirmation of something they had been treating as speculation.

"How do you know that name."

It's not a question.

The Choice Point

The crew has a decision to make in real time. Cut Alaric off hard and withdraw — pretend it didn't happen, watch her recalibrate around what she now knows. Or accept that the conversation has gone somewhere different and decide how much further it goes.

Recovery option: acknowledge the name without explaining the connection. "We've encountered it. We're not in a position to say more." She accepts this because pushing harder right now won't get her further. She is patient. She has time.

If they go further — even partially — skip to Branch 3 from here.

Long-Term Consequences

She now has enough to accelerate her investigation significantly. The crew is inside her mental model of the situation — not just witnesses to disturbances but people with direct knowledge of what is causing them. That changes what she looks for and where.

She will be back. She was always going to be back. Now she comes back with more specific questions, and they will be harder to deflect.

Table note: Alaric's player may feel bad about this. That's the right feeling. Don't let the moment pass too quickly — let the rest of the crew register it. It's good character pressure, not a punishment.

Branch 3 — Loop Her In

The crew makes a deliberate choice to partially read her in. They tell her about the harbor compact, the Watcher, the stakes. Maybe not Scurlock's role yet. Maybe not all three ritual items. But enough that she understands the scale of what she has been detecting.

Her Reaction

She is quiet for a long moment. She looks at the case on the table. Then:

"How long have you known this."

Not accusatory. She is recalibrating everything she has investigated against what she has just been told. The ghost train. The disturbance pattern. The things her organization's sensors have been detecting without understanding. It is all fitting into a different shape than she expected, and she is doing the math in real time.

She will have one or two more questions — specific, precise, the questions of someone building a working model — and then she will be quiet again. Then: "What do you need."

What She Brings
  • Spirit Warden detection resources — she can tell the crew what the sensors are reading near the harbor and the dock district in real time
  • Incident records going back decades — disturbance patterns, anomalies, anything her organization logged without understanding
  • Access to Bellweather Crematorium's oldest records — compact-related deaths, noble house bodies, anything processed in the last two centuries that had unusual spiritual signatures
  • Cover — she can manage the Spirit Warden investigation trail away from the crew, at least for a while
  • Intelligence on Setarra's adjacent network activity that the crew doesn't have
The Complication — Scurlock

Does the crew tell Scurlock? A Spirit Warden in the circle is a significant variable. His reaction depends on whether he thinks she can be managed — and whether he sees a use for her. He might actually want her there. Institutional access is useful to someone orchestrating a ritual transfer. Which means the crew may be handing him a resource they didn't intend to give him.

If they don't tell him: they are now keeping a significant secret from their patron. That has its own weight.

The Double-Agent Dynamic

She is monitoring the crew's progress while feeding information in the other direction — not to her organization, which doesn't know she's here, but to the crew. She is operating entirely alone. If she disappears, nobody is looking for her. That is the vulnerability, and she knows it. She made the calculation and took the meeting anyway.

She is not fully converted to the relational approach — she still has the Spirit Warden instincts, the trained reflexes toward containment and suppression. When something goes wrong, her first instinct will not be the crew's. Managing that gap is a recurring friction.

Clock — "Wren's Cover Holds" (6 segments)

Tick when: she shares Warden resources with the crew; she's observed near the Sunken City by someone connected to an opposing faction; a Setarra cell notices the investigation trail has gotten too specific; she misses a report her organization expected; she actively diverts an investigation away from the crew.

When it fills: someone senior in the Spirit Wardens notices she has been withholding a major incident. What happens next depends on whether the crew has given her anything worth protecting herself with — and whether they are willing to use it on her behalf.

Harrow's Problem With Her

The Fundamental Disagreement

Harrow will not trust her on principle, and his reasons are not wrong. The Spirit Wardens destroy ghosts — efficiently, completely, as a matter of institutional policy. For a Whisper who communes with them, who understands them as presences with weight and history, watching a Spirit Warden process a ghost is watching someone delete something irreplaceable in the name of civic hygiene.

His specific wound is probably something witnessed — a ghost he understood, or a haunting he could have resolved, that a Spirit Warden cleared efficiently. Correctly, by their standards. Completely wrong, by his. He hasn't forgotten it.

Wren is not those Spirit Wardens. She is also not free of what they taught her. She will eventually do something, or say something, that confirms to Harrow that she will always fall back on containment when the pressure is high enough. Whether that moment comes before or after she does something genuinely useful is the dramatic question.

Table note: Do not resolve this tension. Let it be a persistent friction. Wren should earn Harrow's respect through specific actions, not through a single "I'm not like the others" speech. He should come to that conclusion, not be told it.
