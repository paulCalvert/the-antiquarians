Session 8 — The Party

Three scenes in sequence: Wren arrives at the Sunken City, Scurlock reveals the third component, and the crew runs the Dalmorrian Estate — a social score on the front side and an infiltration on the back.

Before You Start

Confirm Before the Session
  • Has Alaric filled the Anya courting clock? One action remaining as of last session. If he fills it before the party: Fork A (room is warm, Schieven relieved, Quellins expecting an announcement). If not: Fork B (harder, richer, Quellins confused, Schieven controlled-furious). Know this before the session starts.
  • Victor is not present. Adjust as needed if this has changed.
  • Crew split: The party requires a front team (social) and a back team (infiltration). Anyone can be on either side — Flint, Castia, Harrow, Brick, Alaric. The back team's obstacles resolve differently depending on who went down.
  • Scurlock: If the crew invited him to the party during Scene 2, he arrives in Beat 7 of the front side. If not, he does not appear.

Session structure: Wren is short — 20 to 30 minutes, a single conversation with branches. Scurlock runs longer — 45 to 60 minutes, with the Q&A being the open-ended portion. The party is the session's main body. The crew split happens early; both sides run simultaneously in the fiction. Intercut if the table is engaged in both; otherwise resolve one side then the other.

Scene 1 — Wren

Full reference: warden-encounter.html. Key updates for Session 8 are here.

How She Comes In

She doesn't announce herself. She comes in like a regular customer — looks at something on the shelf, waits for the shop to clear, then says to Marge: "I need five minutes with whoever runs this place."

Her opening line before anyone can speak: "I'm not here about the uniform."

She knows it's here. She has known for a while. She has chosen not to report it. She is announcing that choice as an opening offer — not a threat, not leverage, a signal. She operates outside procedure when the situation requires it. This situation requires it.

What she wants: a working relationship with people who have been inside whatever is happening in the harbor. In exchange: she manages the Spirit Warden investigation trail away from the crew, shares what the Wardens' sensors are detecting, and holds the uniform case in indefinite suspension.

The Three Branches

Branch 1 — Tight-lipped: She reads the room, doesn't push. She leaves a contact card — a location and a code phrase. "If you find something you can't handle alone, that's how you reach me." She goes. The card is now the crew's to use on their terms.

Branch 2 — Alaric blurts: She says something that lands too close — Setarra's name surfacing in adjacent files, or the pattern suggesting obligation-based activation. Alaric's face changes half a second too late. Her reaction: stillness. "How do you know that name." Not a question. Recovery option: acknowledge the name without explaining the connection. If they go further, treat as Branch 3.

Branch 3 — Loop her in: They tell her enough — the harbor compact, the Watcher, the stakes. She's quiet for a long moment. Then: "How long have you known this." She recalibrates everything she's investigated against this new frame. Then: "What do you need." She brings sensor data, Bellweather records, cover. The complication: does the crew tell Scurlock they now have a Spirit Warden in the circle? He would have thoughts about that.

Harrow's Problem With Her

He will not trust her on principle, and his reasons are not wrong. Spirit Wardens destroy ghosts efficiently and completely. A Whisper who communes with them sees a Spirit Warden processing a ghost as someone deleting something irreplaceable in the name of civic hygiene. Wren is not those Spirit Wardens — but she is not free of what they taught her either. Do not resolve this tension. Let it be persistent friction. She earns his respect through specific actions, not a speech.

The Parting Shot

On her way out — delivered without turning back, at the door:

"When you see Victor — tell him we'll be in touch."

She leaves. The crew is left with: she knows who Victor is. She connected him specifically to whatever she's been tracking. She may already know more about Victor's situation than the crew does.

Aftermath — Schieven's Letter

Shortly after Wren leaves, a letter arrives for Alaric. Schieven's hand — terse, formal.

"You will attend this Saturday. The Quellins will be present. This is not a request."

No pleasantries. No explanation beyond that. This is the setup for Scene 3 — the crew has until this Saturday. That is not long.

Clock — "Wren Knows About Setarra" (6 segments)

Begins empty. Fills based on what branch played out and what Wren is able to learn independently. Tick when: she makes a connection the crew hasn't made yet; she finds evidence that links the Ash Compact to the harbor compact; she hears Scurlock's name connected to the crew; she witnesses something at the harbor she cannot explain. When full: she has enough to move without the crew — and her next step may not align with theirs.

Scene 2 — Scurlock

Setup — Jeeves Summons the Crew

A note from Jeeves — not Scurlock. Precise, formal, unsigned except for the wax seal. A time, a location: the Dalmorrian tower, north quarter. "Lord Scurlock requests the crew's attendance at their earliest convenience." Which means now.

The tower. Old stone. One of six lightning barrier towers in Doskvol, this one in slow disrepair for decades. The Dalmorrian tree crest is carved above the entrance arch — same symbol as Alaric's signet ring, but this version is older and fuller. More leaves. The trunk detailed in a way the modern version isn't. He's seen this crest in the sanctum wall, in his ring, and now here.

Above the arch, worked into the stone of the lintel: a faded partial inscription. Only the first word survives clearly: DALMO—. The rest has worn away.

Beat 1 — The East Wing

Scurlock is already inside when they arrive. He has been preparing the space. As the crew enters, he is mid-task — clearing a surface, straightening something, moving items that don't strictly need moving. He is also dusting. Continuously. A cloth in hand, working over every horizontal surface as he moves around the room. The dust comes back immediately. He notices. He keeps going.

Someone asks about the state of the tower, or the east wing specifically, which is partially collapsed.

"That section has been unusable since... approximately 140 years ago, I lost the thread of when exactly."

He continues dusting. He does not appear to register what he just said. He says things like this occasionally.

The harbor is visible through a broken section of wall. Scurlock positions himself near it when he speaks of important things, as though the view is relevant. It is.

Beat 2 — The Coin + The Speech

He asks for the coin. He receives it. He examines it for exactly one moment — holds it, turns it once — and sets it on the stone with care. Then the tension leaves his shoulders in a way it hasn't since the crew has known him. Not gratitude. Relief. The specific relief of a man who has been holding something painful for a very long time.

He dusts the shelf beside where he placed the coin. The coin sits in a ring of clean stone. The rest of the surface immediately begins to grey again.

He asks about the sanctum — the cultists, the bloodline ward, the condition of the equipment. These are the questions of someone who has been trying to get into that room for a very long time and never could.

Then, almost casual: "I heard about your escapades."

He means the demon. He does not ask how. He moves on to the speech.

He turns to face the crew properly. He sets down the cloth. This part matters.

"The compact is the source of the problem and the solution to it. The original arrangement bound six houses to obligations of protection — spiritual obligations, ones that were reinforced every generation whether the families understood what they were maintaining or not. The arrangement still holds. It can be renegotiated."

"Setarra's power in this city comes from obligation. Every arrangement she holds gives her leverage she can call in. The compact inverts that — binds her to something she cannot easily unpick. Makes her the one with obligations to fulfill rather than debts to collect. But to do that, the compact needs to be activated properly. Three components. Legal instruments of legitimacy, not objects of power."

What the Speech Actually Means

Every word technically true. The compact can be renegotiated — he means: transferred to Setarra. Once she holds the compact, she is bound by its obligations. He has never said the crew is working against Setarra. He has said they are making her the one with obligations to fulfill. This is accurate. He has not said what those obligations are or that the crew is the instrument of her acquiring them.

Players hear: we are activating a legal weapon against Setarra. He means: we are making her the legal heir of an obligation that will constrain her over a century. Both are true. The second is what he intends.

Beat 3 — Three Components, Dalmorre, and the Eel Pie

He lays out the three components:

  • The Reliquary — secured. The first component, already in hand.
  • The Watcher's Coin — the item just retrieved from the sanctum. An object tied to the entity that holds the bay compact. Not the Watcher itself, but something from before it became what it is now.
  • The Compact Document — original founding terms, signed by all six bloodlines. Scurlock believes it still exists, filed in the Dalmorrian family's private archive. The document is what makes the ritual legally coherent. Without it, the other components are just objects.

He walks to the wall. His hand rests near the carved arch inside the tower — six house seals along its inner face, arranged like a ledger column. Three intact. Three cracked through.

"Six bloodlines originally. Three houses have dissolved — their seals no longer carry binding weight. Three remain: Dalmorrian, Strangford, and one I am still confirming. The compact document bears all six original seals. Three will be cracked there as well."

He moves to the window. His glance passes across a point on the Doskvol skyline — a gap where something should be. A demolished tower. It lasts one second. He looks back at the crew.

Alaric asks about the name. Or notices it on the lintel. Or connects the fuller crest here with his own simplified version.

"House Dalmorre." He says the name the old way — Dalmor, clean, no extra syllable. "One of the six. The modern name is a gradual corruption — the family began presenting themselves as part of broader Doskvol nobility rather than as compact signatories, and the name drifted accordingly. The compact document uses the original. The bloodline, however, is entirely coherent."

Alaric connects it. Dalmorrian. His family. His bloodline is the third intact line.

Scurlock waits. He lets that land. He does not rush it.

Then, as the moment settles — he begins to describe where the archive likely is, a particular family estate, a gathering that presents a natural opportunity for access. He is clinical about it. He is walking them toward the party without saying so directly.

And somewhere in this description, describing the household, the private collection, the wine cellar, the below-grade storage — he mentions eel pie. Specifically. As though this is a known detail about the estate. He catches himself a half-beat too late.

"...the lower levels include a wine cellar and — ANYWAYS."

He does not explain the eel pie. He moves on. The crew may or may not pursue this.

The Eel Pie

He has been to this estate. He knows the household. He knows specifics that a patron who has merely researched the target would not know. He has been there, likely more than once, likely when the Dalmorrian family was still called Dalmorre and the parties were different. He does not want to explain this. The crew may or may not connect it to anything. If pressed on how he knows about the eel pie: "I have conducted extensive research on the property." Delivered with the specific dignity of someone who is lying badly and knows it.

Beat 4 — Open Book (Q&A)

Something shifts. Whether it is the Dalmorrian reveal landing, or Alaric's face, or something Scurlock observes in the room — he stops. He looks at the crew properly.

"You have questions. Ask them."

He means it. He will answer three before he closes again. He dusts while he waits. He dusts while they think.

Open Book — The Answers (3-Segment Clock)

Each question asked fills one segment. When the clock fills at 3, he closes. Pick up the cloth again, change his bearing, the window opens and shuts: "I think that covers it." The remaining questions get deferral or misdirection, not answers.

The Ghost Train: "An experiment. Ash Compact — Setarra's organization. They were attempting to build a self-sustaining transit line powered by bound ghost energy. The binding failed. The experiment failed. It made a considerable mess. You handled it more efficiently than I expected." The last sentence surprises him slightly.

The Watcher: "Old. Older than the city as it currently exists. It was human once, in the way that some things that have been other things for long enough still carry the shape. It is bound to the harbor. The compact is what gives that binding its terms." Pause. "The coins — the Tide Tithe — are an echo. An older ritual that predates the current compact. The city has been maintaining a relationship it no longer fully understands. It has been doing so faithfully." He says this as though it is a fact and also something sad.

House Dalmorre: If they ask about the name shift — he smirks. Just briefly. As though the question pleases him in some way he is not going to explain. Then the answer above. The smirk is the tell that this is personal knowledge, not research.

Setarra: "A deep-water entity with centuries of accumulated obligations. She holds debts and arrangements across most of Doskvol's factions and noble houses — not dramatically, not visibly, just structurally. If you removed her cleanly from the city's fabric, a surprising number of things would stop functioning as expected. She prefers arrangements that cannot be easily unpicked. The compact is precisely that kind of arrangement. She wants it." He does not say what she would do with it. He does not mention the bay. He does not give anything that suggests the crew's work is delivering it to her.

His Age: "A specific number would be misleading. I have been present for a considerable period." He turns the cloth over in his hands. "I find the question less interesting than it appears."

The Emperor: His face changes. Something closes. "He was a student of mine. Long before any of this." A pause that is not a pause — it is a door shutting. "What became of him afterward is not a topic I discuss." The clock does not fill for this question. He answered it. The clock fills for things that cost him something, and this one did.

Beat 5 — The Exit

The Q&A closes. He picks up the cloth again. He's already moving — back into the work of preparing the space, or simply restoring distance, or both.

"I have work to do."

He means: this meeting is over. He has said what needed to be said. The rest is theirs to execute.

If no one invites him to the party: he may say, just before they're out the door — "Enjoy the party." He does not look up when he says it. He goes back to dusting.

Optional — The Party Invitation

If someone invites him. Or if he surfaces the information about the estate in a way that implies he might appreciate the access.

He stops what he is doing. He does not turn immediately. Something happens in his expression that he does not quite manage to contain — a fraction of warmth, a moment of something that is almost eagerness, which is immediately recognizable as out of character and which he immediately notices.

"I could... attend. If access would be useful."

A beat. He is catching up to himself.

"For operational purposes."

He goes back to dusting with slightly more focus than the task requires. The cloth moves in precise, controlled strokes. He is not going to bring it up again. If the crew confirms: a small, controlled smile. He catches himself. He looks out the harbor window instead.

Jeeves will need to be consulted about the outfit.

Scene 3 — The Party: Setup

The Estate — Ground Floor

Grand Entrance Hall — wide staircase ahead (off-limits, staff redirect). Coat room left. Staff at door and inside.

Main Reception Hall — high ceilings, long windows, several dozen guests. Waitstaff circulating. String ensemble. Two fireplaces. Garden doors at far end, closed but unlocked.

Dining Room — adjacent through wide archway. Set for dinner, currently empty of guests. Natural space for private conversation.

Schieven's Office — side corridor past dining room. Heavy door. Private.

Servant's Corridor — east wall, runs parallel to main rooms. Access panel, unobtrusive. Route to the lower level.

Garden Doors — far end of reception. Beyond: dark garden, stone path, separate cellar entrance around the side of the building.

The Estate — Sub-Level

Cellar Access — stair behind heavy door in servant's corridor, or garden entrance outside. Unlit below the first landing.

Wine Cellar — first room at the bottom. Occasional staff patrol, one person every 15–20 minutes.

Workroom Corridor — past wine cellar, heading north. Household storage on either side. Lighting minimal.

The Locked Door — midway down. Heavy iron, standard lock. Not alarmed.

The City's Door — further north. Not a ward. Not a trap. The city's first attempt at contact with the crew.

Dirmot's Position — side room just before the archive corridor. Survey equipment. He did not expect company.

The Archive Room — north end, stone room. Oldest part of the sub-level. Centuries of Dalmorrian records.

The Two Forks

Fork A — Alaric fills the Anya courting clock before the party. The formal purpose is fulfilled. The room is warm. Schieven is relieved — which presents as slightly less stern than usual. The Quellins arrived expecting an announcement and are getting one. Better cover, less friction throughout.

Fork B — Alaric does NOT fill the clock. The Quellins arrived expecting a formal announcement. There is none. The room is polite and confused. Schieven is furious and concealing it. Every beat is harder. The Quellins are watching Alaric all evening trying to figure out what kind of man he is.

Fork B is harder but richer. Run it fully if the clock isn't filled. Don't apologize for it.

The Crew Split

The back team slips away during the early party — servant's corridor or garden doors, whichever is less watched. The front team's job: be visible, be present, keep the room from noticing what's missing.

The pressure clock: Dinner will be called upstairs. When it is, the servant's corridor closes off and guest lists get checked. The back team needs to be finished and surfaced before that happens.

Alaric stays front. Eyes/Victor is not present this session.

Front Side — The Social Score

Beat 1 — Arrival

Fork A: The doorman has Alaric's name. The senior Quellin — the father, warm, invested — clocks him from across the room and gives a satisfied nod. Something confirmed. Good start.

Fork B: The doorman has Alaric's name. The aunt clocks him. Her expression is careful and assessing. The cover story is that this is simply a social occasion. Nobody believes it. Nobody says so.

Either fork: Jared finds Alaric within three minutes. He is thrilled to see him. He is not reading the room.

Beat 2 — The Office

Early in the party — before Alaric has settled — Schieven finds him. "A word."

He leads Alaric to the private office. Jared is already there, sitting by the window — the look of a man who was told to wait and has been waiting patiently without quite understanding why. He brightens when Alaric enters.

Fork A — The Briefing: Schieven is controlled. He has won the opening move.

"The Quellins are pleased. I want to keep them that way. You will be attentive tonight. You will not disappear. You will not do anything that reminds anyone in that room that you have been spending your time—" A pause. "— pursuing other interests. The family needs this arrangement. You understand that." Not a question.

Fork B — The Dressing Down: Schieven does not waste time.

"The Quellins came here expecting an announcement. You did not give me one to make. I do not know what you have been doing with your time. Whatever this hobby is — it ends. Tonight you are a Dalmorrian. Tomorrow you begin conducting yourself like one."

Both forks — Jared: From the chair by the window — described, not voiced — Jared, brightly, clearly not tracking the gravity of anything: finger guns. "He's fine! He's already faced down a demon and come out the other side!"

Schieven goes very still.

In Fork A: processing two things — direct demon contact, and the Quellins' zero-tolerance clause. His voice, very quiet: "What."

In Fork B: already furious, so the stillness is colder. He looks at Jared: "Your uncle Jared always turned out alright too. No matter what he did. No matter what it cost." Jared gives a thumbs up. He does not clock what that means.

Both forks end the same. Before Alaric can respond, Schieven locks it down. They are at a party. Lower, for Alaric only: "You cannot turn out like your uncle. Do you understand me? Not tonight. Not ever. Get back out there." He opens the office door. The party sounds drift back in.

Schieven is the voice in this scene. Jared is described. One NPC speaks; the other reacts.

After the Office — Jared

He finds Alaric. He is warm and curious and proud in his easy, uncomplicated way. He wants to know how the demon thing went — genuinely impressed, thinks Alaric is remarkable, says so without any understanding of the weight of it.

This is the man Flint's parents loved. The crew can feel it.

Beat 3 — Jared Released

Jared is a social heat source. Wherever he goes, he raises the temperature. He will: tell the demon story again to someone who absolutely should not hear it; introduce crew members to Quellin family in ways that require immediate damage control; wander toward the Ash Compact associate if no one intercepts.

Managing Jared is an active task. Someone on the front team needs to keep partial attention on him at all times. Not dramatic — just ongoing. Like keeping an eye on a beloved golden retriever at a formal event.

If no one manages him: He finds the Ash Compact associate before Harrow does. The Meridian Circle gets their five minutes.

Beat 4 — Mira Quellin

The family's designated skeptic. Not hostile, just precise. She has been watching Alaric since he walked in. She finds a moment to corner him pleasantly. Drink in hand. A smile that doesn't quite reach her eyes.

Her questions, in order of increasing sharpness:

  1. "What exactly keeps you so busy these days? Your father mentioned you have quite a range of interests."
  2. "And your associates — the ones I've been introduced to tonight — how did that particular group come together?"
  3. "You seem like a capable young man. I wonder sometimes whether capable young men know when they're in over their heads."

Not accusing. Calibrating. Every answer tells her something — content, speed, eye contact, whether he redirects or engages.

Fork A: She is confirming a good bet. If Alaric performs well, she relaxes. Verdict delivered indirectly: "Anya speaks well of you."

Fork B: She is doing damage control. If Alaric stumbles, she excuses herself and finds her brother. That conversation is worse.

The crew can help: Someone engaging Mira separately buys Alaric time. Scurlock, if present, is the nuclear option — she cannot resist interrogating a living Lord Scurlock and abandons everything else immediately.

Beat 5 — Anya

She finds her moment with Alaric. She steers him toward the dining room or a quiet corner. She is efficient.

Fork A: "You came. That means something. I just want to know what it means to you." Not asking for a performance. If Alaric is honest — even partially — she opens: "I know your people are doing something tonight beyond attending a party. I'm not asking what. I'm asking: is it going to be a problem for my family?" She means The Hive, the zero-tolerance clause on supernatural entanglement.

Fork B: Sharper. "I'm going to ask you something and I want a real answer. Not a Dalmorrian answer. What is actually happening tonight?" She has clocked too many things — the crew's body language, Schieven's controlled fury, people slipping in and out.

Same offer, either fork: Give her something true, she helps. Give her nothing, she watches — and she will figure it out on her own eventually. If she's brought in, she becomes an asset: occupies Mira, runs interference with Schieven, covers for disappearances. She knows this room better than the crew does. She is not the enemy. She is a variable that becomes an asset if handled honestly.

Beat 6 — Harrow + The Ash Compact Associate

(Only if Harrow is on the front team)

At some point — main room, quieter corridor, near Jared — Harrow and the associate find each other. Mutual recognition. Immediate. The associate is here for Jared. Close to finalizing the arrangement that would bring him into the ritual. They have worked too long on this.

Associate, low: "Do. Not. Mess this up for me."

Three outcomes:

  • It escalates: Both removed from the party by staff — quietly, firmly. Jared sees it happen. Whatever warmth he had for the Meridian Circle curdles. Clock fills.
  • It stays cold: The associate backs off. Jared stays in play. Clock doesn't fill — but Harrow knows exactly who is working this room and what they want.
  • Sideways: The crew exposes the associate to Schieven or the Quellins. An Ash Compact operative targeting his brother at his son's engagement party — Schieven ends that conversation permanently. Clock fills. Jared is safe. Schieven is now carrying one more thing he will not discuss.

If Harrow is in the back: The associate reaches Jared. The clock does not fill this session.

Beat 7 — Scurlock Arrives

(Optional — only if invited)

The party is well underway. Something on the front team is going wrong — a conversation tipping, Mira pressing too hard, a staff member moving toward the wrong corridor.

Doorman: "ANNOUNCING — LORD SCURLOCK."

The room stops. A wave of silence moving through the guests. There is a living Lord Scurlock? Whatever was about to go wrong is no longer the most interesting thing in the room.

Scurlock enters. Composed. Jeeves clearly intervened on the outfit. He scans for the crew. He also looks, at moments, faintly rigid — posture slightly stiff, like a man sitting near a sound he cannot quite locate. The convergence beneath this house is worse than he expected. He does not comment on this.

He finds the crew. Up close he is uncertain — too many people, too many looking at him, not sure what to do with his hands.

"I finished the work earlier than expected." Beat. "Is there food? I was told there would be food."

The crew deploys him: Point him at Mira Quellin — she abandons Alaric immediately. Have him occupy Schieven with old Dalmorre reminiscences. He follows clear instructions. He does not volunteer himself as a tool but will be used if asked.

Someone corners him: An older noble with an agenda — grievance, old debt, a request. Scurlock is too polite to simply leave and too proud to ask for help. He becomes increasingly rigid. If the crew extracts him, he is quietly, genuinely grateful. He will not say so.

Both can happen in sequence: he starts as a liability, the crew bails him out, they point him somewhere useful. That arc earns something between them. Play both things simultaneously — the composure and the uncertainty underneath it. He has not been genuinely welcomed somewhere in a very long time. Let that show in small moments.

Beat 8 — Dinner Is Called

The hard deadline. The room reshuffles — people move toward the dining room, staff close off the servant's corridor, guest lists get checked against seating. Anyone not accounted for becomes conspicuous.

Stalling options:

  • Jared asking Schieven a long wandering question about family history — requires nothing, completely in character, buys real time
  • Scurlock (if present) approaching the host family for a formal introduction — nobody rushes that
  • Anya (if she's been brought in) quietly telling staff the host needs another moment — she has standing to do this tonight
  • Alaric requesting a brief delay with the Quellin parents — plausible in Fork A, very risky in Fork B

When the back team surfaces: Garden exit, coat room, or wrong turn. Someone on the front needs a cover story ready the moment they appear.

The House Below — Environmental Details

The Pale Roads

The party is happening above a convergence point. The pale roads — channels of spiritual current running beneath the city — intersect under this house, anchored by the bloodline's compact obligations. They have been stable for centuries. Most guests feel nothing.

Ghost-field sensitive characters feel a faint, persistent buzz — low pressure at the edge of perception. Not painful. Not dramatic. The closest analogy: a tone played just above the range where you can identify it as a tone. You feel it more than hear it. Easy to ignore. Not quite ignorable.

Harrow (if front side): Feels it from arrival. By the second hour it is mild, persistent discomfort — background noise with no source. He will recognize what it is. He may or may not mention it to the crew.

Scurlock (if present): Also feels it. He has felt variations of this his entire life. Faintly rigid at moments — the posture of someone with a tooth just beginning to ache, not bad enough to mention. It is worse here tonight than he expected. He does not comment.

Lay guests: Nothing. Whatever is happening beneath the floor does not reach them.

Castia — The City Reaching Out

The city does not know how to speak to a non-Whisper. It is trying things. If Castia is on the front side, small things happen around her over the course of the evening — individually unremarkable, collectively not:

  • She trips on a rug that should not have caught her shoe. As she begins to fall, a floorboard rises just enough to catch her. A servant nearby apologizes. The board is firmly nailed. It did not move.
  • She sets her drink on a side table and reaches for it a moment later. It has slid two inches toward her hand. No incline. No explanation.
  • A wall sconce flickers as she passes — not randomly, rhythmically. She turns to look. It stops.
  • The garden doors swing open briefly, bringing in cool night air, then close again. No wind. She is the nearest guest.

These are nothing, individually. But Castia will begin to feel like the house is paying specific attention to her. She is correct. It is not threatening. It is desperate.

Brick — The Water

If Brick is on the front side, at some point he picks up a glass. The condensation on the outside is moving upward. He sets the glass down. Picks it up again. Normal. He does not mention this to anyone.

Back Side — The Infiltration

Beat 1 — Entry + The Wine Cellar

The stair down is dark past the first landing. The back team's first task: establish the patrol rhythm before committing further.

The patrol: One staff member, roughly every 15–20 minutes, fetching bottles. A good bluff (lost, wrong turn) gets them waved upstairs without incident once. A second encounter is harder.

By crew: Flint reads the rhythm in one pass — non-event. Castia handles it cleanly. Harrow manages, takes more time. Brick: if timing goes wrong, the bluff works once, but staff will remember him.

Upstairs fix if needed: Jared requesting a particular bottle by name — perfect, completely in character. Buys a full patrol window.

Beat 2 — The Workroom Corridor + The Locked Door

Past the wine cellar, heading north. The air gets older. The lighting gets thinner. Midway down: the locked door. Heavy iron. Standard lock.

By crew: Flint — open quietly in under a minute, non-event. Castia — spy background covers basic locks, takes longer, stays quiet. Harrow — not his skillset, slow, risk of noise. Brick — can force it, that is loud, possibly heard by staff near the servant's corridor.

Alternative: The key. Either on the patrolling staff member (ring at their belt) or hanging in the first workroom off the corridor. Finding it costs time but avoids noise entirely.

Beat 3 — The City's Door

Further down — another door. This one should be unlocked. This is not an obstacle. It is an attempt at contact.

If Castia is below: She reaches for the handle. It locks before she touches it — not a click, not a mechanism. It simply closes, the way something closes when something with intent decides it should be shut. She waits. It unlocks. She opens it and steps through. The door swings shut behind her and locks. She tries it from the other side — still locked. She pushes again. It creeps open.

The door is not blocking her. It is signaling. There is no ghost here — she can check and will find nothing. Whatever is doing this is not a trapped spirit, not a ward. It is something that does not yet know how to make itself understood and is trying different shapes. The city is not trying to stop them. It is trying to speak to someone who might listen.

If Harrow is below instead: The door does not lock. The corridor goes wrong. A sound with no source. The sense of something very large becoming aware of exactly where he is standing. He gets through. The city did not reach for him the way it reached for Castia. But it noticed him.

If Flint or Brick is below: The door sticks. Mechanical. They force it or find the reroute. The uncanny element is lost on them. It accumulates elsewhere.

A side passage exists as a reroute — back, around, narrower, reconnects north of the door. Costs a beat.

Beat 4 — Dirmot

Just before the archive corridor — a side room, door ajar. Inside: a compact measuring device, electroplasm-based, delicate, quietly humming. Precise instruments laid out on a cloth. Someone doing careful technical work by lamplight.

Dirmot Eckardd. Former partner of Victor's, now occasional hired tech for the Ash Compact. Technician, not a believer. He did not expect company. He is not here for Jared. He does not know what is happening upstairs tonight.

The crew's first instinct: He is here for the document. Same job, different employer. That read does not survive contact with what he is actually doing. His instruments are not for document retrieval. He is measuring something. That is the first confusion.

What Dirmot Is Actually Doing

The pale roads — channels of spiritual current running beneath the city — intersect here, anchored by the bloodline's compact obligations. Setarra wants to know what happens to this convergence when the compact transfers to her. She is not just trying to close the deal — she is already mapping what comes after. Dirmot has a device that reads spiritual current the way a surveyor reads elevation. He was told what to measure, not why. He has chosen not to ask.

Dirmot — By Crew Member

Flint: If being careful and the door is only ajar, there is a real chance he slips by without Dirmot seeing him. Dirmot finishes his work. The crew learns nothing. That information gap costs them nothing tonight. It may cost them later.

Castia: She recognizes Ash Compact work. Equipment unfamiliar — not standard ritual prep, more technical — but the signature is there. She knows this is not about Jared. Her first read — document retrieval — doesn't fit once she sees the instruments. She enters, closes the door, makes clear she knows enough to make tonight difficult for him. Dirmot gives it up: readings of the pale roads beneath the house, handed off tomorrow, no questions asked. He does not know what the readings mean or who ultimately commissioned them. The money was good. The crew now knows something is being done to the spiritual infrastructure here, connected to the Ash Compact, reason unknown. That is not nothing.

Harrow: He feels the convergence before he reaches the door — a low structural resonance, old enough it stopped feeling unusual to anyone not trained to notice. When he sees the device he understands what it is doing. He does not need Dirmot to explain. The pale roads at this node are being surveyed by the Ash Compact. He has guesses about why — compact work leaves traces in the ghost field, a transfer of that scale reshapes local spiritual geography. He is not certain. He carries the question upstairs. It matters. He does not yet know how.

Brick: Something has been wrong about the ground since he came down the stair. He stepped in a puddle in the workroom corridor. For just a moment the droplets drifted upward. When he blinked, they had settled as normal. The moisture on the walls along the north passage — if he stops to look — is moving slowly upward. When he sees Dirmot, the wrongness and the man become connected. Dirmot takes one look at Brick and makes a cost-benefit calculation. He gives it up and gets out of the way. The conversation is short. Brick leaves with the information and the unsettled feeling.

Dirmot — Outcomes

If he finishes his work uninterrupted: Setarra gets her survey of this convergence node. She has a piece of information she expected to have.

If his equipment is taken or his work is interrupted: The measurements are incomplete. Setarra is missing data about this specific node. The crew will not know they won this — not tonight. But she will be working without a piece of her map.

Voice him as: A technician running a cost-benefit calculation, not a true believer. He gives information to make problems go away. He is not heroic. He is pragmatic. He knows he is measuring the pale roads beneath the house. He does not know why the measurements matter or what happens upstairs tonight.

Beat 5 — The Archive Room

North end. Stone. Oldest part of the sub-level. Drier, stiller, older. Walls lined floor to ceiling with flat wooden drawers, each labelled in a small, precise hand. Centuries of Dalmorrian records. Organized for someone who understood the system and is no longer here to explain it.

By crew: Flint — reads organizational logic intuitively, finds it fastest. Castia — methodical, competent, slower than Flint but gets there. Harrow — not built for this, takes the longest, highest risk of dinner being called. Brick — finds it eventually, brute-force, but leaves the room looking like someone searched it. The family will know. Heat later.

The document: Old. Heavy paper, multiple seals down the left margin — six originally, three intact, three cracked. The founding compact terms, written in formal pre-Cataclysm legal language. It looks significant. It is significant.

Beat 5a — The Newspaper

Same section of drawers. Same era. Folded and tucked — not misfiled, placed. Someone wanted it kept here.

If Flint finds it: He is moving through the north wall drawers, focused, quick — and then he stops. The headline names Jared. The factory explosion, the negligence, the victims. Their names. His parents' names. He is holding a newspaper that blames the man the whole crew spent an evening finding unexpectedly warm, the man Flint found strangely familiar in a way he cannot place. Do not narrate his reaction. Set it down and wait. The silence after is the beat. Someone may notice he has stopped. The document is in one hand, the newspaper in the other. He is not moving. Push him to move, or ask what is wrong — either choice has consequences. The job is not done and dinner is coming.

If Castia finds it: Reads it. Does not recognize the names — they mean nothing to her yet. She pockets it as potentially significant and moves on. She will hand it to Flint later, outside the manor, after the job is done. The hit lands then — not with a clock running, but afterward, when there is nowhere to put it.

If Harrow finds it: He knows about Flint's parents. He understands exactly what he is holding. Show Flint now, in the middle of the job — or carry it until after. Neither option is clean.

If Brick finds it: He reads it. Does not connect it to Flint. He will mention it later — casually, upstairs, probably in front of Flint — "found an old newspaper down there, some factory explosion, nasty business." Accidental grenade.

Beat 6 — Getting Out

Document secured. Same corridor in reverse — past Dirmot's room (however left), through the locked door, through the wine cellar, up the stair.

Timing: If the front team stalled dinner, there is a window. If not, the garden exit — back team surfaces through the garden, comes around to the front, uses the coat room as cover.

Cover stories: Garden — fresh air, needed a moment. Coat room — retrieving something, lost a glove. Wrong turn — genuinely plausible in a house this size.

If Dirmot was left conscious: They may hear him moving before they reach the stair. He will finish his work and leave. Whether the front team has been warned and can intercept him depends on whether the back team relayed the intel during the job.
