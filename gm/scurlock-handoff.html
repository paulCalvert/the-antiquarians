Aftermath B — The Handoff

The crew delivers the coin. Scurlock meets them at the Dalmorrian tower. He tells them almost everything.

The Location — The Dalmorrian Tower

Why Here

Doskvol has six lightning barrier towers. All are in poor repair — the compact has been weakening for centuries, and the towers are the compact's physical expression. Three have living bloodlines behind them. Three do not. All are abandoned.

Scurlock chooses the Dalmorrian tower. He says it is for privacy. This is true and not the whole truth. The ritual requires a compact-significant site. He has already decided where it happens. The crew is being walked through the future scene of the crime without knowing it.

What It Looks Like

Old stone, worn smooth by centuries of weather. The Dalmorrian tree crest is carved above the entrance arch — the same symbol Alaric has on his signet ring, but older and fuller, more leaves, the trunk detailed in a way the modern version isn't. He has now seen this crest in three places: his ring, the sanctum wall, and here. The room knows something he doesn't fully know yet.

Inside: high ceilings, collapsed in one corner. Gaps in the stone where the harbor wind comes through. The lightning barrier crackles somewhere above, faint and distant. The harbor is visible through a broken section of wall — Scurlock stands near it when he speaks, as though the view is significant to him. It is.

The floor near the center has a faint discoloration — old stone, older than the rest, slightly different composition. The ritual site, though neither party says so. Alaric has good reason to find this room unsettling. Let him find it unsettling.

The Scene

How Scurlock Opens

He is already there when they arrive. Standing near the broken wall, looking at the harbor. He does not turn immediately. When he does, he looks at the coin first — just for a moment, before he looks at them. Then:

"You made it through."

He says this without further comment. He receives the coin. He examines it for exactly one moment — holds it, turns it once — and sets it on the stone beside him. Perceptive characters: the tension leaves his shoulders in a way it hasn't since they've known him. Not gratitude. Relief. The specific relief of a man who has been holding something painful for a very long time and has finally been allowed to put it down.

He asks about the sanctum. Specific questions: the condition of the equipment, whether the bloodline ward behaved as expected, what the cultists had managed before the crew arrived. These are the questions of someone who has been trying to get into that room for a long time and never could. He doesn't ask how they feel about what they found. He asks what state things were in.

The Misdirection — Answering Their Questions

They will ask three things. Every answer he gives is technically true.

"What is the Watcher?"

"An ancient sentinel. Bound to the harbor before the current city existed — before the barrier, before these towers. Very old. Very patient. The compact is the source of its authority and the constraint on how it exercises that authority."

He emphasizes constraint. Players hear: renegotiating the compact would free a powerful entity. That is not what he means. He means: the compact defines whose authority the Watcher answers to.

"What does the Ledger actually do?"

"It is the instrument of the arrangement. The formal record of what is owed, by whom, and to whom. The compact does not exist without it. With the Ledger and the remaining components, the terms of the arrangement can be... updated."

Updated. He is describing a legal amendment. Players hear: we are activating a sleeping contract. He means: we are transferring ownership.

"How does any of this deal with Setarra?"

"The compact creates binding obligations of protection. The entity that holds it is bound to defend the city against threats that the harbor cannot contain by ordinary means. Setarra's power comes from obligation and secrecy — every arrangement she holds gives her leverage. The compact would invert that. Bind her to something. Make her the one with obligations to fulfill rather than debts to collect."

He never says the Watcher will be deployed against Setarra. He never uses the word destroy. He says the compact creates protection obligations. Under the new compact — held by Setarra — those obligations exist. He never said who enforces them. Players fill in the active verb themselves. That is the trap.

If pressed: "But will she actually be destroyed?"

"The compact does not work like a weapon you aim. It works like a contract. Once the obligations are properly constituted, what happens next follows from the terms. That is why the components matter — they are not objects of power. They are instruments of legitimacy."

Technically: yes. Once Setarra holds the compact legitimately, what happens next follows from the terms — namely, she is bound to water-based obligations she cannot honor, and the compact grinds her down over a century. He has answered the question. He has not answered it in the way they think.

The Candor Moment — Building Trust

Somewhere in the conversation he volunteers one piece of information that feels like real disclosure — something he doesn't have to say. Use when the crew seems to be pulling away or the trust is thin.

"You may have wondered whether I knew your bloodline would open that door. I suspected. I did not want to raise expectations that might not have been warranted."

He says this as though it is a concession. It is the narrowest possible admission — he suspected, not knew; he was being careful, not deceptive. Every word of it technically true. But it is offered freely, which makes everything else feel more trustworthy. One true thing makes the surrounding architecture easier to accept.

The Third Item — The Reveal

"One component remains. The compact requires something that represents the mortal signatories — the human side of the original obligation. Not simply a heirloom. Something that carries the living weight of a bloodline still bound by the agreement. Something the compact itself would recognize as valid."

He looks at Alaric. "Given what you were able to do in the sanctum, the Dalmorrian bloodline is clearly among the three intact lines. I will research what specific form this component needs to take — an object, a document, a ritual act. I'll be in touch."

He does not say what he plans to do with all three components. He does not explain the ritual. He thanks them and ends the meeting. The implication that he has been pointing Alaric at his own family's history from the start should land in the silence on the way home, if not in the room.

If They Push

Direct Questions — How He Handles Them

"Did you know Alaric could open that door?"
He won't lie. He's too careful for a flat lie that can be checked. "I suspected the Dalmorrian bloodline might be relevant to that specific access. I didn't want to raise expectations." Smooth. True. A complete answer that reveals nothing and confirms everything.

"Are you working for Setarra?"
A pause. Longer than usual. "I am working toward a resolution that serves neither of us well in the short term and disposes of her entirely in the longer one. If you are asking whether she has a claim on me — that is precisely what we are working to remove." True. She does have a claim on him. The ritual removes it. He is not lying.

"What's the ritual actually for?"
"To bind a compact obligation to the appropriate party. The Watcher requires legitimate signatories. The components establish that legitimacy." He will not go further than this. If pushed: "Some things are clearer after they're done." The conversation ends there.

Optional — If the Moment Fits

Scurlock has been surprising himself lately by attempting humor. It does not come easily. Use at most one of these per scene, only if the table energy supports it. The important thing is his reaction to his own attempt — he notices it worked, and is confused about why he tried.

Option A — The Arch (His Favorite, He Thinks)

Setup: The crew arrives and surveys the tower — the crumbling state of it, the age of the stone. Someone (probably Alaric) makes a comment about how old it is, or how it must have been impressive once.

Scurlock, examining one of the carved stones: "That arch was considered quite fashionable at the time."

A beat of silence.

"I realize that sentence has implications I did not intend."

He does not elaborate. He moves on. The crew is left sitting with the implication that he was there when it was built. Which is the funny part. Which he knows is the funny part. Which is why he tried it. He is surprised to find he hoped it would land.

Option B — The Eight (Dark, Unintentionally)

Setup: Someone in the crew is being pessimistic about their odds, or expressing doubt about whether any of this is survivable.

Scurlock, without looking up: "I once retained a crew of eight for a task of similar complexity. Extremely capable people."

A pause.

"I still remember most of their names."

He says this as though it is a compliment. He genuinely means it as a compliment. He does not clock the crew's reaction until a moment later, and then he seems briefly uncertain whether he miscalculated. He did not miscalculate. That was a joke. He just doesn't fully recognize the mechanism.

Option C — The Exceptions (Genuinely Trying to Reassure)

Setup: Someone asks whether he's sure the plan will work — directly, a moment of genuine doubt or challenge.

Scurlock: "I have been managing problems of this nature for several centuries. I have an excellent record."

A beat.

"The exceptions do not warrant discussion."

He genuinely believes this is reassuring. He is confused when it isn't. He meant it sincerely. This is perhaps the most revealing of the three — it is not quite a joke, it is Scurlock trying to offer comfort and not fully understanding why the words came out that way.
