Heat, Entanglements & Faction Play

The city's response to the crew — how heat builds, what it costs, and how to keep factions alive between scores.

Heat

The Heat Track

Heat is a 9-segment track measuring how much law enforcement and city authorities are paying attention to the crew. Heat is not a hidden measure — it's a concrete mechanical pressure that shapes how dangerous the world is right now. A crew at 0 heat is practically invisible. A crew at 8 heat is one job away from a serious confrontation with the Bluecoats or Inspectors.

Generating Heat

After every score, assess the heat generated. Starting from 0, add for each applicable factor:

  • +0 — Clean and quiet. No witnesses, no bodies, no attention.
  • +1 — Restricted area entered, or the score was conspicuous or unusual.
  • +1 — Hostile territory — operating in a rival crew's turf.
  • +1 — Well-connected target (noble house, ministry official, faction leadership).
  • +1 — A killing was committed during the score.
  • +2 — A Bluecoat or city official was killed.

Add the total to the crew's current heat. Heat can also accumulate from entanglement outcomes, faction responses, or direct consequences of the crew's behavior between scores.

When Heat Overflows

When heat reaches 9 and must increase further, the crew's wanted level increases by 1 instead and heat resets to 0. Wanted level is permanent until actively reduced (a significant downtime investment or a major favor). Each increase in wanted level makes entanglement rolls worse and patrols more aggressive.

Reducing Heat

The primary way to reduce heat is the Reduce Heat downtime activity. A character bribes officials, plants false leads, keeps the crew off the streets, or spreads misdirection. Roll the relevant action; on a 6 reduce heat by 3, on a 4–5 reduce by 2, on a 1–3 reduce by 1 with a possible complication. A crew that consistently runs jobs without spending downtime on heat reduction will find the situation escalating quickly.

Wanted Level

What Each Level Means
  • Wanted 0 — Unknown. No active law enforcement interest. The crew moves freely.
  • Wanted 1 — Known. The Bluecoats have a file. Entanglements skew worse. Some contacts become uncomfortable.
  • Wanted 2 — Marked. Regular patrols look for them. Some parts of the city are harder to move through openly.
  • Wanted 3 — Hunted. Inspector involvement. The Bluecoats are actively pursuing. Contacts in law-adjacent positions go cold.
  • Wanted 4 — Enemies of the City. Military or Spirit Warden involvement. Operating above ground is extremely dangerous. This is existential.
Reducing Wanted Level

Wanted level doesn't reduce easily. Options include: a significant downtime investment (multiple actions across multiple scores), a major favor from a powerful faction or noble contact, staging an elaborate misdirection that shifts blame elsewhere, or eliminating the specific official driving the pursuit. Each wanted level reduction should require a genuine effort — it represents clearing the crew's name in a city that doesn't forget easily.

Turf

What Turf Is

Turf is physical territory in the city that the crew controls — a street, a market, a district block, a building. Holding turf provides ongoing coin (usually 1 coin per downtime) and narrative leverage (locals know you, you have eyes on the ground, rivals think twice). Turf is also a responsibility — it can be contested, attacked, or become a liability if the crew generates too much heat in it.

Gaining Turf

Turf is gained by taking it from someone else, filling a vacuum when another power leaves, or establishing a new presence in an unclaimed area. New turf should require a score or a significant investment. It doesn't happen passively — the crew has to make a move. When they take turf, decide with the players what they're actually controlling: a criminal operation, protection, legitimate business fronts, or physical territory.

Losing Turf

Turf can be contested by rival crews or factions. If the crew can't defend it — because they're running other jobs, generating too much heat, or simply outmatched — it can be taken. A rival crew making a move on turf should trigger an entanglement or a score in response. The crew's choice of whether to fight for it, negotiate, or let it go says something about their priorities.

Faction Play

The Faction Map

Every significant group in Doskvol — criminal crews, noble houses, law enforcement bodies, the Church, the Ministry — has interests and plans that operate independent of the players. Your job as GM is to keep those factions moving. Between sessions and during downtime, advance faction clocks. Let factions succeed at things the crew didn't stop. Let the city change. The players will notice and will start having opinions about it.

Faction Status

Track the crew's standing with each significant faction on a scale from −3 (at war) to +3 (allied), with 0 as neutral. This isn't rigid — it's a tool for remembering where relationships are. Status shifts based on what the crew does:

  • +1 or +2 — The crew helped the faction, protected their interests, or did a score that benefited them.
  • −1 or −2 — The crew crossed the faction, took from them, or benefited a rival.
  • +3 (allied) — Active cooperation, mutual protection, significant favors done and returned. Takes time to build.
  • −3 (at war) — Hostility. The faction is actively working against the crew. Takes something serious to reach and something significant to reverse.
What Factions Want

The most important question for each faction is: what are they trying to accomplish right now? Keep one or two active faction goals in mind at all times. The Ministry wants expanded port authority. Clelland wants to pin something on Strangford. A rival crew wants the crew's turf in Crow's Foot. When these goals intersect with the crew's activities, interesting things happen. When they don't intersect, the factions advance their goals anyway — the crew just finds out about it later.

Keeping Factions Active

Factions should feel like players in the game, not static backdrop. Between sessions, pick one or two faction moves to narrate — a document filed, a contact approached, a patrol route changed, a threat delivered. These don't all need to be scenes. Most can be established facts at the start of a session: "In the weeks since the last score, the Ministry has posted an inspector at the North Harbor gate. Mordis says customs are taking twice as long." Let the players decide what to do about it.

The best faction moves create opportunities, not just obstacles. The Ministry's inspector at the harbor is a problem — but it's also a potential asset (he can be bribed, blackmailed, or used to misdirect someone), a source of information (he sees everything that comes through), and a story hook (who put him there, and why now?). Every faction move should have at least one interesting angle for the crew to engage with.

Current Faction Priorities

Ministry of Preservation

Actively pushing to expand regulatory authority over the harbor and fleet operations. Using the barren hunting grounds as justification. The noble houses are resisting in Council but haven't found an effective counter yet. Ministry's short-term goal: get an inspector appointed to oversee all outgoing leviathan hunter vessels. This would give them a chokepoint on House Strangford's primary revenue stream. They would use it.

House Strangford — Political Survival

Aware that three Council houses are working against him. Current priority is finding leverage against the Bowmore-Clelland-Rowan bloc — something that breaks one of the three alignments. Rowan's bloodline secret is known to Strangford's occult contacts but he hasn't used it yet. Waiting for the right moment. Also quietly investigating who on the Ministry payroll is feeding the Council opposition's timing.

The Watcher's Compact — Slow Burn

The Drowned Ledger remains with Setarra. The seal situation is unresolved — two Dalmorrian signatories (Alaric and Jared) are alive but don't know it. Setarra is patient. She doesn't need to move immediately; she needs one willing or coerced signatory. Jared is the more isolated target. Until the compact situation resolves, this is a sleeping faction clock — but it's ticking.
