Clocks

How to use progress clocks, threat clocks, and faction clocks to create tension without dictating outcomes.

What a Clock Is

The Basic Tool

A clock is a circle divided into segments — 4, 6, 8, or 12 — that tracks how close something is to happening. When you tick a segment, you advance that thing. When the clock fills, it fires. Clocks are not secret mechanical scaffolding — you can show them to players, describe them as a sense of urgency, or keep them behind the screen. The point is that they externalize time pressure and give partial progress meaning.

Clock Sizes
  • 4 segments — Fast. A chase, an alarm response, a guard patrol cycle. Things that will resolve within the scene if not interrupted.
  • 6 segments — Medium. A typical score complication, a short-term faction move, a recovery from moderate harm.
  • 8 segments — Slow. A complex plan coming together, a major faction project, recovery from serious harm.
  • 12 segments — Very slow. Long-term story arcs, city-scale events, things that won't resolve for several sessions.

When in doubt, use a 6-segment clock. It's fast enough to feel urgent and slow enough to allow player agency. Use 4 when you want immediate pressure. Use 8 or 12 when something should loom in the background across multiple sessions.

Types of Clocks

Progress Clocks — Getting Something Done

Used when the players are working toward a goal that requires multiple steps or rolls. Each success ticks the clock forward. At standard effect, tick 1 segment. At great effect, tick 2. At limited effect, tick half a segment (round up on the next tick). When the clock fills, the goal is accomplished. Use these for complex objectives — cracking a well-guarded vault, persuading a reluctant contact, deciphering an ancient document, completing a ritual.

Threat Clocks — Something Getting Worse

Tick toward a negative outcome. Guards getting closer, an alarm spreading through the building, a wounded character losing blood. Every complication you inflict can tick a threat clock instead of being immediate. When the clock fills, the threat fires — and it should be significant. Threat clocks are visible pressure. Show them. "The patrol is at 3 of 6 — they'll sweep this corridor in a few minutes." That information is part of the game.

Faction Clocks — The World Moving

Track faction plans and projects between sessions. The Ministry of Preservation's push to control the harbor. The Bowmore-Clelland-Rowan bloc's maneuvering against Strangford. A rival crew establishing territory. Faction clocks tick based on your narration of offscreen events — you decide when to advance them, usually during downtime or between sessions. When a faction clock fills, their plan completes and changes the world. Players may or may not have been in a position to stop it.

Racing Clocks — Competing Goals

Two clocks ticking toward opposite outcomes. The players' progress clock racing against a threat clock. The faction trying to complete their ritual racing against the players trying to stop it. Both tick based on actions and circumstances — whoever fills their clock first determines the outcome. Racing clocks turn a single conflict into a sustained narrative with stakes that escalate in both directions.

How to Tick a Clock

Ticking From Action Rolls

The most common case: a player succeeds on a roll working toward a goal. Tick based on effect level. On a 4–5 (partial success), you can tick the progress clock but also tick a threat clock as the cost. On a 1–3 (bad outcome), tick the threat clock without advancing progress, or tick neither and introduce a complication instead. Let the dice create the tension rather than pre-setting how many rolls something takes.

Ticking From Consequences

When you want a consequence to be "things got worse" rather than immediate harm, tick a threat clock. This lets bad rolls accumulate into a payoff rather than hitting players with isolated punishments. "The guard didn't spot you, but the alarm is now at 4" is more interesting than "you take 1 stress" because it creates a problem they'll have to deal with. Reserve immediate harm for when the fiction clearly calls for it.

Ticking From Narration

Between scenes and between sessions, you can tick clocks without a roll. The Ministry files their permit restriction — tick the faction clock 2. The character with the festering wound spent the night in a damp safe house — tick the complication clock 1. The world moves whether or not the players are watching. Narrating these ticks keeps factions and threats feeling alive rather than paused between player actions.

GM Technique

When to Introduce a Clock

Introduce a clock when you want partial progress to matter, when you want to visualize stakes, or when something in the fiction has begun that will conclude. Don't introduce a clock for things that will resolve in a single roll — just roll and describe. The clock earns its existence when there are multiple steps, when players have choices about how fast to move, or when you want to show the world working against them in parallel to their actions.

Showing Clocks to Players

You don't have to hide clocks. Showing a threat clock creates productive anxiety and lets players make informed choices. "The alarm is at 3 of 6 — do you push on or find cover?" is a better scene than a hidden clock that triggers without warning. The exception: some clocks should be surprises, particularly long-term faction moves that the players don't yet know are happening. Use your judgment about what serves the fiction.

When Clocks Fire

When a clock fills, something happens — make it real and make it matter. A threat clock that fills means the guards arrive, the ritual completes, the contact goes cold. Don't soften it after the fact. The clock was visible (or the players knew it existed); the consequence was always the price. What happens next is part of the game. A faction clock that fills changes the city. Note it, describe it, let the players react to a world that moved without them.

Clocks are not punishment — they are narrative structure. A threat clock that fires isn't the GM winning; it's the story generating a new problem for the players to engage with. The clock that fires is often more interesting than the one they prevent.
