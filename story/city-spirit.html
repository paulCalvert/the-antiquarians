The Spirit of the City

Something in Doskvol is beginning to notice. It does not have a name yet. It does not need one.

What the Crew Has Noticed

Background Wrongness
  • Carved faces on building facades that seem oriented differently than they were a moment ago
  • Echoes in corridors and canals that don't match their source — the wrong sound from the wrong direction, slightly delayed
  • The central statue in the Outer Hall of the Sunken Temple: entering, it presented as supplication; leaving, as fear. Both positions visible simultaneously when looked at directly
  • A general quality of being observed in certain parts of the city — not by a person, by the place

None of these things are threatening. They are unsettling in the way that being watched is unsettling, even when whoever is watching means no harm. Something is paying attention. It has been paying attention for a while. It is paying more attention now.

What It Is

A Nascent Civic Spirit

Doskvol has been inhabited for long enough, and has experienced enough concentrated human emotion, belief, and history, that it has developed a nascent spirit of its own. Not fully conscious — it cannot reason, plan, or communicate in language. It reacts. It fears. It resists. It recognizes patterns the way an old animal recognizes danger: not through thought but through something older than thought.

It is not the Watcher. The Watcher is bound to the harbor and has his own ancient obligations. The city-spirit is something different — younger, less formed, more diffuse. It is present everywhere in the city simultaneously, the way a body is present in all of itself at once. It does not have a face, a voice, or a plan. It has only pressure and reaction.

Its Relationship with the Watcher

The city benefits under the Watcher's care. The compact has kept the harbor safe for centuries, and that safety is part of the city's fabric — woven into what it means for Doskvol to persist. The city-spirit does not understand the compact as a legal structure, but it feels the Watcher's presence the way a body feels its own heartbeat. Steady. Known. Right.

What is happening now — the compact being pulled toward Setarra — registers as wrongness at a deep level. Not danger exactly. More like the feeling of something vital being loosened that was not meant to be loosened. The city is afraid in the way a body is afraid of its own bleeding.

Its Relationship with Scurlock

He Is Doing This to It, Not for It

Setarra is not a city problem. She is a Scurlock problem. He made a deal; she owns a piece of him; he wants out. The harbor compact is the mechanism he has found to extract himself. The city is the instrument he is using to do it.

The city knows this. Not in words — it cannot reason. But it registers: this is not being done for me. This is being done to me. The increasing hostility, the wrong echoes, the architectural faces, the statue's fear — these are not atmospheric flavor. They are the city saying no in the only language it has.

Scurlock's response is to pat it on the head and tell it things will be fine. He knows better. He always knows better. He has it all in hand. The city is scared. The city will be okay. He will work it all out in the end.

He was wrong. And the city's stunned silence after the ritual — not fear, not resistance, just the sound of something hurt — is the deepest wound the campaign can deliver to him, because it is the thing he loves most telling him what he cost it.

Its Relationship with the Crew

They Are Scurlock's Instruments

The city-spirit's hostility toward the crew is not personal. They are the moving parts of Scurlock's plan — every time they complete one of his objectives, the wrongness the city is resisting gets closer to completion. It doesn't know the crew. It knows what they're doing.

This could change. If the crew breaks from Scurlock's plan — if they resist, redirect, or actively work to repair the compact — the city's attention would shift. Not to warmth exactly. But to something less like opposition. The city recognizes alignment the way it recognizes threat: not through thought, but through something felt.

The Conductor

The City's Most Active Agent

The Conductor — the spectral figure who vanished from the ghost train before the score ended — is the city's most deliberate expression. Not a ghost in the normal sense. Something the city crystallized around a specific function: keep the compact intact, keep the city intact, resist what Scurlock is doing.

He cannot confront Scurlock directly. Scurlock is too old, too woven into the city's own history — the city knows him the way a body knows an old scar. But the Conductor can work sideways. He nudges Wren toward the crew. He engineers meetings. He tries to put people in motion who might stop what is being set in motion.

Scurlock is aware the Conductor exists, approximately. He considers it a manageable complication. He is, in this as in the rest, wrong.

How It Manifests — GM Seeds

Active Manifestations — Use Sparingly
  • Carved stone faces on building facades oriented slightly toward the crew when they pass, returned to neutral when they look directly
  • Echoes that arrive before their source — footsteps ahead of the walker, voices before the mouth opens
  • Doors in old buildings that stand ajar when they should be closed. Not opened by a person. Just ajar.
  • Sections of old wall that feel warm to the touch in cold weather — the oldest stone, the original city
  • The sensation of being followed in empty streets — not a person, not a ghost. Something structural. The city watching from its own bones.

Use these as background texture, not events. The city-spirit should never demand attention — it should accumulate at the edges until the crew realizes the city has been watching them for a while. One seed per session maximum. None during high-action scenes.
