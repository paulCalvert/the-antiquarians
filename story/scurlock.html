Patron — For Now

Lord Scurlock

Ancient noble. Has a way to deal with Setarra — but he needs the crew to collect the pieces first.

First Contact — The Letter

Delivered Before Their First Meeting
Friend,

What you seek is a reliquary of black basalt veined with leviathan bone ash. It is taller than a coffin, wider than a man's shoulders, and etched in spiraled warding script. The surface bears six containment seals. Three will be fractured. Do not disturb them.

It is not to be opened. It is not to be damaged. It must be removed intact.

The token you acquired will open the door. You must be fully prepared before stepping through.

Regards,
Lord Scurlock

Note what he doesn't say: what it is, why it matters, what he wants it for. Every sentence tells them how to get it. None of them say why.

The Arrangement

How It Stands

Scurlock is an ally helping them oppose Setarra and contain a dangerous occult threat. He directed them to retrieve the Reliquary from the ghost train. He says two more items are needed. He seems to know what he's doing.

Character Notes

  • He knows things about Doskvol that have no business still being known by anyone living
  • Manipulative, patient, intelligent — he is not panicking, which is itself unsettling given the situation
  • He speaks about Doskvol differently than he speaks about people — with a familiarity that suggests the relationship is old and personal
  • Whatever he has done to survive this long, he does not discuss it
  • Ancient — he has been alive long enough to remember the original compact negotiations and the construction of the lightning towers
  • Occasionally attempts humor. The attempts are infrequent, unexpected, and land in ways that confuse him as much as the crew

The Truth — His Actual Long Game

What's Actually Happening

Scurlock is beholden to Setarra. He made a deal he cannot escape — ancient, binding, the kind that doesn't have an exit clause. His plan: collect all three ritual components using the crew as his instrument, then perform the ritual that binds Setarra to the harbor compact.

Here is what he knows that nobody else does: Setarra cannot honor a water-based obligation. She is dust. The harbor compact is water. They are constitutionally, metaphysically incompatible. He is not handing her a weapon. He is binding her to something that will grind her to nothing — gradually, over approximately a century, as the obligation she cannot fulfill consumes the power she gains from holding it. In his calculation, this is acceptable. A hundred years of Setarra as a degraded, compromised custodian of the harbor is better than the current situation. He will be free. She will be destroyed. The city will survive.

He has not told the crew any of this, for one practical reason: they would not accept the timeline. A hundred years of Setarra with harbor authority — things let through, tides manipulated, gradual erosion of the compact's protections — is not a timeline the crew can live with. He has made the calculation for them. He believes he knows better.

What He Gets Wrong

His cold calculation contains an error he doesn't discover until after the ritual: he has underestimated the immediate damage. He told himself the city could absorb a century of Setarra's mismanagement. Watching the harbor shift in real time, feeling the compact wrong-weighted under her hold — he may realize he was wrong. Doskvol may not survive his long game intact.

This is the moment that opens him to working with the crew to accelerate the timeline. Not guilt. Not a change of heart. A miscalculation that he now has the information to correct — if he can bring himself to ask for help from people he used.

Doskvol As Self

The City Is an Extension of Him

He doesn't love Doskvol the way someone loves a place. He identifies with it the way a person identifies with their own body. Ancient, arcane, mysterious, persisting — the qualities that describe the city describe him. He has been here long enough that the distinction has blurred. The city and Scurlock must always persist. No matter what.

The tell: bad things happening to people don't visibly affect him. Bad things happening to the city do. A district fire, a canal collapse, infrastructure failing — these land differently. Not grief exactly. The feeling of damage to something that is, in some deep way, himself.

He doesn't say "I love this city." He would never say that. But watch how he talks about it. He says it persists the way someone talks about a quality they admire in themselves. He says it has survived everything with the tone of a survivor talking about their own survival. He is not describing the city from outside it. He never has been.

The Megalomania

He genuinely believes he knows what is best for Doskvol. He has watched it long enough, loves it thoroughly enough, is entangled with it deeply enough that the idea that the city might know something he doesn't — or want something he doesn't want to give — does not occur to him as a real possibility. He is right. He is always right. He has been right often enough to stop questioning the certainty.

This is why he dismisses the city-spirit's resistance as nervousness to be managed. The city is scared. He understands. He will fix it. He knows better. He has it all in hand.

He was wrong.

The Long Isolation

Why He Stopped Connecting

He didn't decide to stop caring about people. It happened the way slow things happen — gradually, then completely. You make a friend. You watch them die. You make another. After enough iterations, the mind starts doing the math before the heart can commit: this person will be gone in thirty years. Maybe forty. The calculation runs faster than the feeling can form, and eventually you stop noticing the feeling at all.

He has been in Doskvol long enough to have watched generations of people he knew — their children, their children's children — live and die. Every stone in the city is an old friend he can still touch. Every person is a brief candle. He stopped lighting them, or thought he had.

The result is not cruelty. It is a kind of distance that reads as cold and calculating but is actually protective. He is not indifferent to people. He has made himself indifferent to people. There is a difference, and it is the difference that makes him recoverable.

The Dust

A Recurring Visual Motif

Setarra has a hold on Scurlock, and it manifests physically: dust settles on his cape. Not dramatically — just a faint grey film on the shoulders, a residue he can never quite account for. He brushes it away without acknowledging it. He has been doing it for a very long time.

How to Deploy It Across the Campaign
  • First appearances: He brushes dust from his shoulder mid-conversation. Doesn’t stop talking. Doesn’t acknowledge it. If anyone mentions it, he changes the subject.
  • Under pressure: The gesture becomes more frequent. He does it twice in a single scene. A careful observer might notice it correlates with moments when Setarra’s name or plans come up directly.
  • Losing control of the habit: He catches himself mid-gesture and stops. Deliberately. That restraint is more unsettling than the habit itself.
  • The ritual moment: He unclasps the cape and lets it fall. It hits the floor and releases more dust than there should be — a quiet, grey billow. He does not pick it up. He straightens his jacket and looks like himself for the first time. The debt is paid. The dust is gone.

Table note: Don’t call attention to it. Let it accumulate. Players should notice before they understand what it means — and when the cape drops at the ritual, they should feel it without needing it explained.

The Thawing

Something Is Coming Back

Working with the crew over weeks and months, something has been moving in him that he hasn't noticed in a very long time. It is not comfortable. It is not welcome. He is not sure what to do with it.

The moments: he attempts a joke, and it lands, and for a moment he is simply pleased — and then he notices the pleasure and doesn't know what to do with it. Why did he try that? He runs it back looking for the strategic purpose and cannot find one. That is more unsettling than if he had found one.

He reassures one of them — Alaric, after the sanctum, or Brick, after something difficult — and realizes mid-sentence that he is not doing it to keep a resource functional. He is doing it because he doesn't want them to feel bad. When did that start? He finishes the sentence and is quieter than usual afterward. Not cold. Processing.

He does not acknowledge it. To them or himself. The feelings accumulate somewhere he doesn't examine. This will matter later.

Table note: The thawing should show in small moments only — a beat too long before a deflection, a joke that surprises him as much as the crew, a concern that slips through before he catches it. He never names it. The crew should notice before he does.

The Ritual Moment

His Lowest Point

He has done ruthless things before. Centuries of them. Most left no mark because the part of him that registers marks had gone quiet. But this one is different — and the specific reason is Jared. He has watched Alaric. He knows what that uncle means to him. The relationship is exactly the kind of thing he stopped allowing himself: a warm, uncomplicated affection between two people, one of whom would walk into traffic for the other without thinking.

He uses it anyway. Because the city must persist. Because the calculation still comes out the same way it always has.

The ritual completes and he feels something he thought was dead. Not guilt — guilt implies he thinks he made the wrong choice. More like the feeling of someone who has made the right choice and discovers it is not the same as a good one. He is sick of himself in a way he hasn't been in a very long time. And then he looks at the harbor and understands he was wrong about the damage, and the sickness becomes something harder to name.

His Relationship with the City-Spirit

Dismissive Paternalism

The city-spirit is resisting him. It has been resisting for a while — increasing hostility, the wrong echoes, the architectural faces, the statue's fear. The city knows this plan is bad for it. Setarra is not a city problem; she is a Scurlock problem. He is solving his problem with the city's body, and the city knows it.

His response is the response of someone who has told himself for so long that he knows best that the city's fear registers as nervousness to be managed, not information to be heard. It's fine. I've thought this through. You can handle it. You've handled worse. He is not contemptuous of the fear. He just doesn't take it seriously.

The Conductor — the city's most active agent of resistance — is working against his plan. He knows this, approximately. He considers it a manageable complication, the way someone considers a concerned family member a manageable complication when they've already decided what they're going to do.

When the ritual completes and the damage begins, the city's response shifts from fear to something he hasn't felt from it before: a stunned, injured silence. That's the moment the certainty breaks. The city — which is, in some deep way, him — resisted. He ignored it. He was wrong. And the thing he loves most is the thing that is suffering for it.

The Betrayal Design

When and How

The betrayal should land hard but leave room for Scurlock to become a complicated ally again afterward. He is not a villain who wants the city to burn — he is a man trying to survive a deal gone wrong, who convinced himself he had an elegant solution and found out he was wrong about the cost.

After the ritual: the path to an alliance is available if the crew can stomach it. He wants the same thing they want — Setarra destroyed, the compact repaired, Doskvol intact — just on a timeline he has now been forced to revise. He is not seeking forgiveness. He is seeking a corrected calculation. Working with him requires the crew to decide whether a useful, genuinely remorseful, still fundamentally self-interested ancient noble is an asset or a liability.

Connections

  • Setarra — a problem he is navigating very carefully; the source of his entire situation
  • The Drowned Ledger — now in his possession; one of three ritual components
  • The crew — partners in dealing with a mutual enemy, as far as anyone can tell
  • Doskvol — he loves the city as an extension of himself; its suffering is his suffering; he does not fully register that its fear is a message
  • The City-Spirit — resisting his plan; he knows it, dismisses it; this will be the deepest wound when he turns out to be wrong
  • Jared — the intended ritual vehicle; he has watched Alaric enough to know what using Jared costs; he does it anyway
