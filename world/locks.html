Locks of Doskvol

Every culture has a philosophy about security. Here's what it looks like when you're reading a lock.

Cultural Lock Types

Akorosi

The standard. General purpose, widely manufactured, no particular ideology behind them. What you find on most doors in Doskvol. Not elegant, not crude � just functional. A competent lockpick expects these.

Skovlan

Tough material, crude construction, stubborn mechanisms. Built by people who expected the lock to take a beating. No flourish, no complexity � just mass and resistance. You won't finesse a Skovlan lock. You wait it out or you force it.

Iruvian

Needlessly complicated and artsy. Fine detail work, decorative flourishes that are also functional, mechanisms that seem to exist to show off. The challenge isn't strength � it's complexity. A locksmith made this to prove something. You have to appreciate it before you can beat it.

Dagger Isles

Looks simple. It isn't. Usually 2�3 concealed locks operating as a single mechanism � think puzzle box. Each lock must be engaged in the correct sequence or the others reset. You don't pick a Dagger Isles lock; you solve it. Hard to crack without knowing the logic first.

Severosi

Very rare � not common in Doskvol. If you pick it manually, it takes a long time. But it's built around sound: the correct pitch whistle opens it almost instantly. Instructions are typically written on the lock itself in Hadrathi. If you can read Hadrathi and carry a tunable whistle, these become trivial. If you can't, expect to be there a while.

Tycherosi

Always weird. Always arcane. The mechanism has nothing to do with conventional lockpicking — there are no tumblers to set, no pins to lift. The lock opens when you do the right thing to it. Instructions are etched into the lock itself in Hadrathi.

Simple Tycherosi locks use a single Hadrathi word. The word is the instruction. Blood — open a vein on your palm and press it to the face of the lock. Fire — hold a flame to it until it clicks. Water, ash, breath, iron — each is exactly what it sounds like. If you can read Hadrathi, a simple lock takes ten seconds. If you can't, you're guessing at a ritual you don't understand.

Complex Tycherosi locks use a riddle. A full sentence or two in Hadrathi, usually oblique — the answer to the riddle is the method. "What feeds on living warmth but dies when given it freely?" Answer: fire. You light the lock. A riddle lock is meant for someone who knows the language and knows how to think like a Tycherosi maker. Getting it wrong typically does something unpleasant.

A Tycherosi lock is less a security mechanism and more a ritual requirement — a test of whether you belong in the space it protects.

Imperial

Highest quality. The hardest to crack of any conventional lock. Almost always requires 3 keys operated simultaneously � single-key variants exist but are rare and considered inferior. Used on vaults, government installations, and anything the Empire decides needs protecting. If you're staring at an Imperial lock without all three keys, you're going home empty-handed or you're blowing the door off.

Hadrathi

The arcane script used on Severosi and Tycherosi locks. Not widely spoken or read in Doskvol. A Whisper or an academic might know it. A Leech who's worked with Tycherosi components might have a passing familiarity. Flint would recognize it and know what it means � whether he can read it is a separate question.

The Lock Club

Possible Hook � Flint

There exists � somewhere in Doskvol � a loose network of lock enthusiasts. Pen pals. A book club with better toys. They correspond about mechanisms, trade rare specimens, and meet occasionally to attempt particularly interesting locks together. The kind of people who would look at an Imperial triple-key mechanism and think: beautiful.

Flint has the obsessive temperament for this. It's a downtime hook, a contact network, and a source of both rare locks and rare knowledge. The right member of this club might know where to find a Severosi specimen � or have seen an Imperial lock that shouldn't exist somewhere it shouldn't be.
