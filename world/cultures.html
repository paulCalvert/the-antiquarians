Cultures of the World

The peoples who built Doskvol, fled to it, trade through it, and haunt its streets.

How to Read This

Doskvol is a port city at the edge of the known world, and it shows. Imperial administrative culture is the official layer — the law, the currency, the formal language of power. Beneath that is everything else: Skovlan refugees who came after the war, Iruvian merchants who never left, Dagger Isles sailors on their third season, Tycherosi who came for reasons nobody discusses. Every district has a different mix. Every crew operates in the seams between them.

These are broad strokes — stereotypes that hold often enough to be useful, not rules. Any individual will be more complicated than the summary.

The Empire

Akorosi — The Default

The Imperium speaks Akorosi. The law is written in Akorosi. The currency, the forms, the official correspondence — all Akorosi. As the dominant culture of the empire, Akorosians in Doskvol don't particularly think of themselves as having a culture; their customs are just how things are done. The noble houses are Akorosi. The City Council is Akorosi. The Church of Ecstasy is an Akorosi institution.

In Doskvol specifically: Akorosi culture skews mercantile and hierarchical. Old names matter. The right school, the right accent, the right address in Brightstone. There is a lot of performance involved in being properly Akorosi in polite company. In Crow's Foot or Nightmarket, it's just background noise.

Imperial Architecture & Civic Life

The empire is old enough to have built over itself multiple times. Imperial civic architecture tends toward heavy stone, formal symmetry, and the aggressive use of administrative lettering — laws, dedications, and warnings carved into facades as a matter of course. The post office, the tax office, the Spirit Wardens' processing stations — all share the same visual grammar: authority communicated through mass and permanence.

Daily life is organized around the electroplasm economy. The barrier towers require regular maintenance draws. The city's power grid runs off leviathan blood. This is not background detail; it structures the working week, the labor market, and the class system in ways most residents stopped noticing generations ago.

The Neighbors

Skovlan — The War, The Exodus, The Grief

Skovlan is — was — a confederation of independent city-states to the north. Doskvol fought a long, grinding war with them over annexation; the Imperium won. The peace settlement was brutal. What followed was worse: a massive influx of Skovlan refugees, displaced soldiers, and economic migrants into Doskvol and other Imperial cities.

Skovlanders in Doskvol are a generation or two deep now. The first wave came with nothing and took the worst work; their children grew up in Coalridge and the Docks, working the refineries and the hull gangs. They are often immediately identifiable — the northern features, the accent that doesn't fully fade, the Skovlan clan tattoos that older immigrants still carry. There is ambient anti-Skovlan sentiment in the city, particularly in more Imperial-identified circles, that ranges from casual dismissal to open hostility.

Skovlan culture: fiercely communal, deeply suspicious of Imperial authority, organized around clan bonds rather than formal institutions. Skovlanders trust people who've proven themselves over institutions that claim legitimacy. They keep debts — owed and owing — with long memories. The idea of dying without those balanced is genuinely troubling to them.

Iruvian — The Traders, The Scholars, The Long Game

Iruvia is a collection of city-states far to the south — warmer, drier, older in a different way than Doskvol. Iruvian merchants have been in Doskvol for a very long time. They came for the leviathan trade routes and stayed because the city needed what they brought: a banking system that actually functions, luxury goods that Akorosi manufacture can't replicate, and a merchant class sophisticated enough to navigate Imperial bureaucracy from the outside.

Iruvians in Doskvol tend toward the merchant and professional class — import factors, jewelers, money-lenders, scholars at the Academy. They cluster in Silkshore and the trade districts near the docks. The Iruvian Consulate maintains a formal presence and is one of the few foreign institutions the City Council treats as a genuine peer rather than a tolerated nuisance.

Iruvian culture: highly aesthetic, patient, indirect. Confrontation is a last resort and a sign that something has already gone badly wrong. Relationships are investments; gifts and favors are logged as carefully as ledger entries. Iruvians make excellent partners if you understand that the long game is always being played — and poor enemies for the same reason.

Dagger Isles — The Free Sailors, The Quiet Merchants

The Dagger Isles are a scattered archipelago between Doskvol and the open Void Sea — independent city-states that have so far resisted Imperial annexation through a combination of geography, naval capability, and a calculating neutrality that refuses to make enemies it doesn't have to. Their ships are in every port. Their merchants are in every market. They sell to everyone and make formal alliance with no one.

In Doskvol: Dagger Isles traders and sailors make up a significant chunk of the independent merchant fleet. They're found in the dockside taverns, the chandlers' shops, and the cargo brokerages. The Dagger Isles Consulate is notably well-funded and active in city politics — specifically around trade agreements and the leviathan hunting licenses that the Ministry of Preservation has been tightening.

Dagger Isles culture: self-reliant, pragmatic, allergic to formal authority. The free-sailor tradition prizes competence over lineage. What you can do matters more than where you're from. They have a reputation for being difficult to read — not because they're deceptive, but because they solve problems sideways rather than directly and expect you to follow the logic.

Severosi — The Scholars of the Cold

Severos is a remote northern region, colder and more isolated than anywhere the Imperium has successfully administered. Severosi who appear in Doskvol are almost always scholars, specialists, or exiles — there isn't much economic migration from a place that has almost nothing to migrate toward. The language, Hadrathi, shows up in specialized academic and arcane contexts; it was apparently the dominant scholarly script before the Cataclysm and retained that position in isolated northern institutions.

Severosi are rare enough in Doskvol that most residents have never met one. When they do appear, they tend to move in academic or occult circles. They have a reputation for being deeply formal, intensely curious, and very hard to read emotionally — which may simply be a cultural difference in how feelings are expressed.

Tycherosi — The Ghost-Touched

Tycheros is a land where the boundary between the living world and the ghost field is thin to the point of being permeable. The people who come from there carry that with them in ways that are occasionally visible: heterochromia — one eye a different color, sometimes a color that doesn't occur in normal irises — and an ambient relationship with the spirit world that ranges from heightened sensitivity to something more direct. Some Tycherosi have partial ghost ancestry in their bloodline. Most don't discuss the specifics.

In Doskvol, Tycherosi cluster in Silkshore and the older canal districts. They have a reputation for occult knowledge, unconventional problem-solving, and a comfort with the ghost field that most Akorosians find unsettling. Spirit Wardens regard them with particular attention. Tycherosi tend to regard Spirit Wardens with something between amusement and contempt.

Tycherosi culture is hard to generalize because the population in Doskvol is a self-selected group: people who left a strange place and came to one of the most haunted cities in the empire. What they share is a matter-of-fact attitude toward death and the dead, a nonlinear approach to problems, and a strong preference for handling their own affairs without institutional involvement.

The Cataclysm's Shadow

The World Before

The Cataclysm — whatever it was — ended the age that came before it. The sun went dark. The ghost field merged with the living world across most of the surface. Leviathans emerged from the Void Sea in numbers that made open travel impossible. The cities that survived did so behind lightning barriers; the cities that didn't are ruins in the Deathlands, or nothing at all.

Every culture in the current world has a "before" that none of them fully remember. Iruvian scholarly tradition maintains the most intact pre-Cataclysm records; Hadrathi was apparently the common academic script of that era. Skovlan oral history carries fragments. Akorosi official history emphasizes the founding of the empire and the construction of the barriers, treating everything before as a dark age from which the Imperium rescued civilization.

What that age actually looked like — what the cities were, how people lived, what the world was before the sun went out — is genuinely uncertain. The Cataclysm erased the record almost completely. What survived did so in basements, locked rooms, and the memories of things older than the event itself.

What It Means Now

The current world has no living memory of sunlight. This is simply how things are. Children grow up knowing the electroplasmic glow of the barrier, the oil lamps and lightwell systems, the carefully maintained growing operations in Barrowcleft. The concept of crops grown under an open sky is something read about in old texts, not a thing anyone alive has experienced.

The psychological weight of this is mostly invisible because it is universal. Everyone lives in the dark. Everyone has always lived in the dark. What shows up instead is a cultural orientation toward persistence — the thing most celebrated in Doskvol is not thriving, exactly, but enduring. Carrying on. Still being here. The great boast is that the city has never fallen. The unspoken implication is that everything else has.
