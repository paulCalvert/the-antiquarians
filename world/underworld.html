The Criminal World

How crews operate, what heat means, and what it costs to work outside the law in Doskvol.

What a Crew Is

Organized Crime, Doskvol Style

A crew is a small criminal organization — not a gang in the street sense, but a working unit with a specialty, a territory, a reputation, and a set of relationships with the city's factions and power structures. Crews operate on scores: discrete jobs with a target, a plan, and a payout. They maintain a home base, cultivate contacts, and build influence over time. The difference between a crew and a gang is focus and professionalism. A gang holds a corner. A crew holds a specialty.

Tier

Every criminal organization in the city operates at a tier — a rough measure of resources, reach, and reputation. Tier I crews are local operations scraping by. Tier IV organizations effectively control entire districts and have relationships with noble houses. The Antiquarians are currently building toward the middle of this scale. Tier matters because it determines who will deal with you, who will ignore you, and who will try to absorb or destroy you. Moving up requires either accumulating enough coin and influence that the next tier treats you as peers, or eliminating competition to fill the vacuum they leave.

Scores

How Jobs Come In

Scores come from three directions: contacts who bring work to the crew (a fence who needs something retrieved, a faction lieutenant who needs a problem solved), faction commissions where an allied organization officially asks for something, and self-initiated work the crew identifies and plans independently. Self-initiated scores are the most profitable and the most dangerous — no one is vouching for the information, and the crew absorbs all the risk of being wrong about the target. Contact-sourced work is more reliable but comes with obligations. Faction commissions are steady and political.

What the Payout Actually Looks Like

Coin is the obvious payout, but scores also produce rep — reputation that changes how factions and contacts perceive the crew. A well-executed score for an allied faction builds the relationship. A mess that draws too much Watch attention costs it. The best scores accomplish the goal, produce coin, build rep, and generate no new problems. These are rarer than anyone admits.

Heat & Wanted Level

Heat

Heat is the city's attention on the crew. Every score generates some — even a clean job leaves traces. A messy score that involved killing, fire, or significant collateral damage generates considerably more. Heat accumulates locally: the Watch in a particular district starts asking questions, informants get nervous, contacts become cautious. High heat means that routine operations are riskier, trusted contacts may distance themselves, and the Bluecoats are looking for any excuse to pick someone up. Heat can be reduced by lying low, bribing the right people, or doing a favor for someone with Watch connections.

Wanted Level

Where heat is local and operational, wanted level is how seriously the law has formally committed to finding the crew. A low wanted level means patrols have a vague description. A high wanted level means the Inspectors are involved, warrants have been issued, and the city's incorruptible investigative arm is actively building a case. Wanted level is much harder to reduce than heat — it requires either significant political favor, a legal maneuver through a sympathetic magistrate, or doing something that gives the authorities a reason to quietly drop the matter. Reaching the highest wanted levels is essentially a countdown to Ironhook.

The Inspectors do not take bribes. The Bluecoats do, but their cooperation is temporary — pay them once and they will expect to be paid every time. Heat managed through bribery is heat that is rented, not resolved.

Turf & Territory

How Factions Control Space

Criminal factions in Doskvol control territory the way noble houses control social circles — through demonstrated presence, relationships with the local population, and credible willingness to defend what they claim. Turf is not primarily physical (anyone can walk down a street); it is relational. A faction that controls a district has informants in the taverns, an understanding with the local Bluecoats, relationships with the merchants who want protection, and enough muscle on call that competing interests think twice. Operating in another faction's territory without permission or tribute is possible. It is also a statement.

Operating in the Gaps

The crew's current position in Nightmarket means operating in a commercially busy district with competing interests and no single dominant faction. This is useful — no single organization has grounds to demand tribute, and the social density makes anonymity easier. It also means no single organization has particular reason to protect the crew from problems. Nightmarket's neutrality is a resource. It is also a vulnerability.

Criminal Social Codes

What You Owe

The underworld runs on debts, favors, and reputation. A crew that pays its debts, protects its contacts, and does not create more problems than it solves is worth doing business with. A crew that burns contacts, defaults on obligations, or leaves a mess that implicates the people around them gets a reputation that closes doors. There is no formal court for this — reputation enforces itself. A contact who was burned will tell others. A faction that was wronged will remember when the crew needs something. Trust in the underworld is expensive to build and cheap to lose.

The Unwritten Rules

Certain things are understood: you do not target civilians without reason — the Watch cares more and the community turns hostile. You do not touch a crew's lair without expecting total war in return. You do not reveal a contact's information to the law regardless of what's offered — this rule in particular is absolute, because the entire network depends on it. You do not move against an allied faction without formally breaking the alliance first, because ambushes from supposed allies create a climate of paranoia that damages everyone. None of this is written down. All of it is enforced.

Ironhook

The End of the Road

Ironhook Prison in Dunslough is where the city puts people it has decided to stop dealing with. It is run by the Bluecoats and conditions inside reflect their leadership accurately. A sentence in Ironhook is not rehabilitation — it is removal. People with money and connections rarely see the inside of it; their charges are managed, reduced, or redirected into fines and house arrest before trial. People without those resources go to Ironhook and stay until someone pays for them to leave or their sentence ends. Getting a contact out of Ironhook is a legitimate score in itself. The Bluecoat officers who run it are open to arrangements, but the arrangements are expensive and the officers' memory of who paid them is selective.

The Antiquarians

Thieves

The Antiquarians are a crew of thieves — specialists in acquiring things that don't belong to them, from places that don't want them taken. The antique shop front in Nightmarket is their public face: a legitimate-appearing business that provides cover, occasional actual income, and a reason for a range of unusual visitors to come and go without raising obvious questions. The crew's specialty is objects with history — things that have value precisely because of what they are or what they're connected to. This has put them in contact with the city's occult layer faster than most new crews expect. Thieves in Doskvol work adjacent to that world whether they choose to or not. The crew has not chosen to avoid it.
