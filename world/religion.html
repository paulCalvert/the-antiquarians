Religion & The Church

What people believe, what they practice, and what waits beneath the doctrine.

The Church of the Ecstasy of the Flesh

Public Doctrine

The state religion of the Imperium. The Church teaches that the body is the seat of divine experience — that life, sensation, and physical existence are sacred gifts. The flip side of this doctrine is its abhorrence of what it calls "corruption": spirits that linger in the world rather than passing on, ghosts that cling to the living, the walking dead. The Church's theological position and the Spirit Wardens' practical function align neatly: both hold that spirits belong beyond, not here. In practice, the Church and the Spirit Wardens maintain an uneasy mutual respect rather than genuine cooperation.

The Sanctorium

The grand cathedral in Brightstone is the center of civic religious life in Doskvol. Its tower is one of the tallest structures in the city. Attendance at major Sanctorium services is expected of the noble and professional classes — not legally required, but social consequence for notable absence is real. The Sanctorium operates charitable programs, arbitrates certain civic disputes, and serves as neutral ground for negotiations that would be too dangerous to hold elsewhere. The senior clergy hold considerable soft power in Brightstone's social world.

In the Streets

Below Brightstone, the Church's presence is thinner. There are local chapels in Nightmarket, Charterhall, and the better parts of Crow's Foot — small storefront affairs with part-time clergy who are often also practicing another trade. Working-class citizens interact with the Church primarily for births, deaths, and the occasional saint's day market. The Church's charitable operations in the poorer districts are real but underfunded and run mainly by lay volunteers. Ordinary people in Charhollow or Dunslough are more likely to light a candle to the Weeping Lady than to make the trip to the Sanctorium.

The Church maintains that the Cataclysm was a consequence of humanity's hubris — specifically, the pre-Cataclysm civilization's attempts to transcend the body's limits through supernatural means. The irony that the entire city now depends on ghost energy for survival is not lost on the clergy. They navigate this tension through careful theological language about "sanctioned" vs. "unsanctioned" use of spirit energy.

The Weeping Lady

The Street Faith

Not affiliated with the Church of the Ecstasy. The Weeping Lady honors a historical figure — Lady Devera, a noblewoman who allegedly gave away her entire fortune to the poor of Dunslough and died in poverty herself. Whether this story is literally true is debated; that it is believed completely in Charhollow and Dunslough is not. Her image — a woman with downcast eyes and open hands, weeping — appears chalked on walls, carved into building corners, and stitched onto clothing throughout the poorest districts. Her followers maintain the only reliable food kitchens and shelters in Dunslough. They ask nothing in return. This is sufficiently unusual in Doskvol that the charity itself has become a form of devotion.

The Church of the Ecstasy tolerates the Weeping Lady cult rather than endorses it — technically the Lady is a saint within their calendar, but the street practice has drifted far enough from Church doctrine that the clergy in Brightstone find it uncomfortable. In Charhollow, nobody cares what the Brightstone clergy think.

The Forgotten Gods

Before the Cataclysm

Before the Cataclysm, the peoples of the Shattered Isles worshipped a different pantheon entirely — older gods, more varied, tied to land, sea, and the cycles of seasons that no longer exist. The Cataclysm and the subsequent rise of the Imperium effectively ended this religious tradition. The Church of the Ecstasy was a rising movement before the Cataclysm; the catastrophe gave it both a theological explanation (divine punishment) and a power vacuum to expand into. The old gods' names survive mostly as curses, street names, and fragments of folk tradition. Scholars debate whether they were real entities, cultural constructs, or something stranger.

Survival in Practice

Certain communities — particularly Skovlan immigrants, Dagger Isles sailors, and the older canal-adjacent neighborhoods — maintain quiet private traditions that predate the Church. The Gondoliers' practices have roots in pre-Cataclysm maritime religion that the Church was never quite able to fully absorb or suppress. These are not organized movements. They are grandmothers who know which corner to leave offerings at, sailors who know the right words before the lightning barrier gate opens, canal workers who know that certain water spirits need to be acknowledged or they become problems.

What Lies Beneath the Doctrine

The Inner Order — Ascension

The Church of the Ecstasy has a public face and a secret one. The inner order — accessible only to high-ranking clergy and wealthy initiates — pursues a doctrine of "ascension": the belief that demons are perfect, immortal beings, and that it is possible for a human to become one through ritual transformation. This is not widely known and would be a significant scandal if it were. Lord Strangford is a high-ranking adept. The cathedral's catacombs host ongoing experimental rituals. The outer Church has no idea this is happening — or, more precisely, those who have found out have not spoken about it again.

The Path of Echoes

Lord Penderyn's clandestine society operates in direct philosophical opposition to the Church's doctrine on ghosts. Where the Church says spirits must pass on, the Path of Echoes actively seeks communion with trapped ones — maintaining that the accumulated wisdom of the long-dead is the most valuable resource in the city, and that the Spirit Wardens' policy of destruction is a civilizational loss. Membership is deliberately elite: nobles, senior scholars, wealthy dilettantes with occult interests. The Spirit Wardens are aware of the Path of Echoes and consider it a standing priority. The society has survived because its members are individually too prominent to move against quietly. This may not last.
