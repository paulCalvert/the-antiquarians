The Deathlands

What lies beyond the lightning barrier. What it has always been. What it is becoming.

Beyond the Barrier

What the Towers Keep Out

The lightning barrier is not a wall — it is an electroplasmic field maintained by the ring of towers around the city's perimeter, strong enough to repel the entities that would otherwise flood in from outside. The towers run continuously. When a tower goes dark, the Sparkwrights respond faster than the Watch responds to murders. The barrier is the city's most critical infrastructure, and everyone who understands how it works treats it accordingly.

The World Outside

Beyond the barrier, the world is what the Cataclysm made it: dense with unmoored spirit energy, populated by entities that are not human, and hostile to unprotected life. The pre-Cataclysm landscape — forests, farms, roads, the ruins of cities — still exists in fragments. The ruins are inhabited now. Not by people. The spirit wells that form spontaneously inside Doskvol form constantly and aggressively outside it, where there is nothing to contain them. Wandering through the deathlands unprotected is understood to be a death sentence. It is not technically impossible to survive. The difference between the two statements is meaningful.

The Cataclysm's Legacy

Roughly 850 years ago something broke the Gates of Death — the metaphysical boundary that allowed spirits to pass from the living world to whatever lay beyond. No one currently alive knows exactly what was done or who did it. The theories range from deliberate ritual to catastrophic accident to divine punishment. What is known: in the immediate aftermath, the sun nearly went out, the ocean transformed into the Void Sea, and the dead stopped moving on. Eight centuries of accumulated spirit energy has made the deathlands what they are today. The process has not stopped. The density of the ghost field outside the barrier has been measurably increasing for the last century.

What Lives Out There

Horrors

The term "horror" covers a range of entities that are neither human ghosts nor physical creatures. Some are spirits so ancient or degraded they bear no resemblance to human origin. Some appear to be native to the ether rather than displaced from life. Some are large enough to dwarf buildings. Their common characteristic is electroplasmic density — they are concentrated nodes of spirit energy, which makes them dangerous to the living and destructive to the physical world around them. The lightning barrier repels them because it runs the same type of energy at sufficient intensity to make approach painful and entry lethal. This is also why leviathan hunters face horror encounters during harvests: the blood they're extracting attracts anything in the vicinity that feeds on electroplasm.

The Walking Dead

In the deathlands, corpses do not stay still. Without the Spirit Wardens' intervention and the crematorium's furnaces, a body in the deathlands rises within hours. What rises depends on the circumstances of death, the local ghost field density, and factors scholars don't fully understand. The resulting entities range from mindless echoes to aggressive specters to something worse. The deathlands outside the barrier are populated by centuries of accumulated risen dead from whatever settlements once existed there. Most of the ruins are their territory now.

Travel Between Cities

How It's Done

The other cities of the Imperium — Iruvia, the Dagger Isles, the cities of Akoros, what remains of Skovland — are reachable, but not easily. Travel routes between cities use two methods: sea routes along the Void Sea's coast (faster, but navigating the Ink Sea requires leviathan hunter experience and purpose-built vessels), and overland coach roads. The overland roads run between chains of lightning relay stations — smaller barrier installations that create protected corridors through the deathlands. Travel between stations is done in armored coaches built to withstand electroplasmic discharge. The schedule is fixed, the coaches are expensive, and they do not stop between stations regardless of what is happening outside.

The Relay Stations

Each relay station is a fortified waypoint: barrier towers, a garrison, stables, and a lodging house for travelers waiting out the window between coach departures. Some have grown into small permanent settlements with their own economies. Most are bleak outposts staffed by people who have made a specific kind of peace with their circumstances. The stations are Imperial installations — maintained by the Crown, garrisoned by Imperial soldiers, and subject to the same authority the Lord Governor answers to. They are not subject to any city's local law.

Traveling outside the barrier without the coach road infrastructure — on foot, through open deathlands — is not standard criminal behavior because it is not standard survival behavior. Circumstances that would push someone to attempt it are by definition extreme. People do attempt it. Fewer return than set out.

The Shattered Isles

Where Civilization Still Stands

The Imperium spans several regions, each with surviving cities under their own lightning barriers. Akoros is the Imperial heartland — Doskvol is its largest city, and the Lord Governor answers ultimately to the Imperial administration there. Iruvia is a hot, arid region to the south, known for its merchant culture, elaborate social hierarchies, and distinctive lock-craft — Iruvian locks are among the most complex in the world. The Dagger Isles are a loose archipelago of island city-states, maritime culture, and independent traditions that the Empire absorbed without fully homogenizing. Skovlan was the last free nation — now occupied, its people scattered across the Imperium as laborers, soldiers, and refugees carrying a culture the Empire has not yet finished trying to suppress.

What This Means in Doskvol

The city's population reflects this map. Iruvians in Doskvol tend toward the merchant and skilled-artisan class. Dagger Isles immigrants are heavily represented in maritime work. Skovlans are everywhere, doing the labor the city needs and receiving the least for it. Tycherosi — from the northern city of Tycheros, known for their particular occult traditions and distinctive physical appearance — are a smaller community but disproportionately represented in certain criminal and arcane specialties. Every cultural group brought their own locks, their own customs, and their own relationship with the dead with them. Doskvol contains multitudes, and most of them are trying to survive the same city.
