The Harbor & Waterways

Canal culture, the blood trade, and whatever lives in the black water beyond the barrier.

The Canals

How the City Moves

Doskvol is built across multiple islands and peninsulas � the districts are separated by waterways. Canal culture is older and slower than the streets above: more intimate, more dangerous in its own way, and operating on its own set of rules. The canals serve for personal transport, commercial goods, waste disposal, fishing, and smuggling. Canal approaches to buildings bypass street-level Bluecoat patrols entirely � an advantage that has not gone unnoticed by those who need it.

Canal eels are resistant to the city's pollution and are a staple protein across working-class Doskvol. Eel pies are what you eat when you live near the water. The Gondoliers have been pulling corpses from these canals since before the Spirit Wardens were organized. The two facts are not unrelated.

The Gondoliers

Ancient Guild, Deeper Knowledge

The canal boat operators' guild � ancient, proud, and considerably stranger than they appear. Headquartered at the Thricebar Canal Locks. Organized by district, with each canal area maintaining its own chapter. They were managing canal ghosts long before the Spirit Wardens were established by the Emperor. They know which canals are haunted, which waterways pass under spiritually active buildings, and where ghost doors open over the water at predictable times.

Who People Actually Trust

Most ordinary citizens bring "weird problems" to the Gondoliers rather than official authorities. The Gondoliers are more trusted on supernatural matters than the Spirit Wardens � they are local, they are not anonymous, and they have been dealing with whatever the canals produce for generations. They are said to have accumulated occult secrets over centuries: "Many things are submerged in Doskvol." Whether they will share what they know depends entirely on whether you are someone worth sharing with.

The Docks

Ancient District

The Docks predate the Cataclysm � among the oldest structures still standing in Doskvol. Hub of all cargo trade, fishing, and leviathan hunter operations. Culture is nautical, rough, and pragmatic: dock workers, sailors, longshoremen, fishmongers.

The Dockers

The longshoremen of Doskvol are organized under the Dockers � a labor faction with genuine power over what enters and leaves the port. They load and unload cargo vessels, operate the cranes and winches, and manage the warehouses along the waterfront. Because everything the city consumes passes through their hands, a Docker work stoppage is an event that makes noble houses nervous. They know this and have used it. The Dockers are allied with the Grinders � both are working-class labor factions with significant Skovlan membership, and both carry the same long memory of what the Empire did to Skovland. They are not soldiers and not sailors; they are the people who make the harbor actually function, and they are underpaid for it by design.

Leviathan Hunting

The Blood Trade

The leviathan hunter fleet is the foundation of the city's electroplasm economy and therefore of all other power. Each noble house owns vessels; Strangford's is the largest on the continent. The ships are titanic iron and steel hulks, purpose-built for void sea navigation � not wooden vessels. The hunt proceeds by harpoon and cable, with small teams climbing onto the living creature's back to operate industrial drills that pierce the skin and pump the blood into holding tanks. Leviathans are truly immortal � they cannot be killed, only harvested from. Hunters have never seen more than a fraction of one's full body.

On the Water During a Harvest

Ghosts are attracted to leviathan blood � manifestations during harvest operations are common. Demons can spontaneously appear around the blood. The creature's body is slippery; crews drown regularly. If the leviathan dives, ships can be pulled under. Leviathan hunters are among the most experienced workers in Doskvol at managing supernatural threats in real time, because they have no choice.

The hunting grounds are going barren. The leviathans are migrating or disappearing � the cause is unknown. The city's electroplasm supply is at risk, and the Ministry of Preservation is using this crisis to argue for government control over the fleet. The noble houses are not taking this well. The situation is unresolved.

The Void Sea & The Barrier

What's Out There

The water surrounding the Shattered Isles is no longer ordinary ocean. After the Cataclysm it turned opaque black � the Void Sea, sometimes called the Ink Sea. It is possible to see what appear to be stars or cold lights far below the surface. What they are is not agreed upon. Hunters who have spent years on the void sea report different things. None of the reports are reassuring.

The Lightning Barrier

Rings of lightning towers � each over two hundred feet tall with internal generators � produce a continuous electroplasmic field around the city. Maintained by Sparkwright engineers. Without it, the deathlands' horrors would consume Doskvol within days. Every leviathan ship that leaves must pass through a gate in the barrier. The moment of departure is the moment the city's protections end. Most hunters perform a private ritual at the gate. What ritual varies by crew and by faith. That they perform one does not.

Water Culture & Folk Belief

What Dockworkers Say

The harbor has its own oral tradition, passed between dockworkers, fishermen, and canal laborers in the way these things always pass: casually, as though everyone already knows. The sayings cluster around the same themes — debt, the water's patience, things best left alone.

  • "Pay the Tithe and the water leaves you be."
  • "Don't whistle on black water." (And separately: "Whistle for the water and the water listens." Both are true. Context matters.)
  • "If the harbor gives something back, leave it."
  • "If your coin comes floating back, don't go out tomorrow."
  • "The deep doesn't forget. It just waits."
Gondolier Rituals

Gondoliers are the most deliberate practitioners of harbor folk tradition. Their rituals are not religious in any formal sense — they are operational. Things that work. Things that have always been done because the alternative was tested once and the results were bad.

  • Touch the rail before boarding — always with the left hand
  • Toss a coin over the left shoulder, not the right, when crossing certain canal junctions
  • Mutter before crossing water channels that run under old buildings — the words vary, but the muttering is consistent
  • Never name the fare before they're aboard — say "a passenger" until they've crossed the first lock
  • Leave the last pole-stroke unfinished when docking at night — let the canal do the final movement
Physical Evidence

The harbor's folk tradition leaves marks throughout the dockside districts. Anyone who looks knows what to look for.

  • Small coin piles wedged into the cracks of rotting dock posts — some recent, some so old the coins have corroded into the wood
  • Anchor shrines: old iron anchors propped in canal-side alcoves, offerings left at their bases
  • Barnacle-encrusted handbells mounted under certain dock structures — their original purpose is debated; nobody removes them
  • Chalk tide marks on canal walls with symbols no one currently alive knows the meaning of, re-marked whenever the tide washes them away
  • Knotted rope hung in threes from dock supports in the older sections of the harbor — specifically three knots, specifically an odd number of loops

None of the people who practice these rituals necessarily believe in them in any formal sense. They would describe them as habit, as custom, as "what you do." The Gondoliers would say only that the canals have always been this way and they work with what is. Whether any of it is connected to something older and more intentional — something that remembers being tended — is not a question most dockworkers think to ask.
